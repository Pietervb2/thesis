\subsection{Modeling assumptions}
One of the key modeling assumptions is that the hydraulic and thermodynamic systems can be treated as decoupled. First, the hydraulics calculation is completed, resulting in a mass flow, which is then used for the thermodynamics. The speed of sound in the water is (1481 m/s at 20 $^{\circ}\text{C}$ \cite{speedofsound}), causing the changes in flow rate and pressure to be within seconds in a small district heating network such as a DCS. In contrast with the spread of thermodynamic changes traveling at the speed of the maximum flow rate, which is around 2 m/s. This difference in time scale between the thermodynamics and hydraulics validates their decoupling \cite{KUNTUAROVA}:

Furthermore, the following assumptions are applied throughout this thesis project \cite{MAURER2021244,sibeijn2025economic,KUNTUAROVA,PipePDE}.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The water is incompressible. 
    \item The fluid flow is fully developed.
    \item The pipes are cylindrical and are filled with water.
    \item The fluid flow is one-directional. 
    \item The fluid velocity and temperature are spatially homogeneous throughout the cross-section of the pipeline.
    \item Minor pressure losses are neglected.
    \item No water leaves the pipe system.
    \item Homogeneous mixing at the pipe junctions.

    \item Frictional heat is neglected.
    \item The diffusion of heat in the axial direction is neglected.
    \item Material properties of the water, pipe and insulation are constant in time and uniform in space and temperature independent. 
    \item Thermal interaction between the supply and return pipes is not included.
    \item The ambient temperature is constant along the length of the pipe.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Dimensionless Numbers}
\textbf{The Reynolds Number (Re)} is the primary parameter correlating the viscous behavior of all Newtonian fluids \cite{white2011fluid}. It is the ratio of the internal to the viscous forces within a fluid. And it is used as an indicator of whether a flow is laminar or turbulent. 

\begin{equation}\label{eq::Re}
R e=\frac{\rho D v}{\mu}=\frac{4 \dot{m}}{\pi D \mu}
\end{equation}

Where $\rho$ [kg / m$^3$] is the density of the fluid, $v$ [m/s] the fluid velocity, and $D$ [m] the diameter of the pipe, $\mu$ its dynamic viscosity [kg / (m s)], and $\dot{m}$ [kg/s] is the mass flow. 

\textbf{The Prandtl Number} is the ratio between momentum diffusivity and thermal diffusivity. It is used in heat transfer and fluid dynamics to characterize the relative thickness of the momentum and thermal boundary layers. It can be determined using the following relation: 

\begin{equation}
    Pr = \frac{\mu c_p}{k}
\end{equation}

Where $c_p$ [J / (kg K)] is the specific heat capacity and $k$ [W /(m K)] is the material's thermal conductivity. 

\textbf{The Nusselt Number (Nu)} represents the ratio of convective to conductive heat transfer across a boundary. It is characterized by the relation below:

\begin{equation}\label{eq::Nusselt}
    Nu = \frac{h D}{k}
\end{equation}

With $h$ [W / (m$^2$ K)] as the convective heat transfer coefficient and $D$ [m] as the pipe diameter. 

\textbf{The Darcy friction factor (f)} can be found in the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which calculates the pressure flow drop through a pipe using the pipe flow. The coefficient can be determined using the Moody diagram, where $f$ is plotted against the Reynolds number for different relative roughness values $\epsilon / d$. While the Colebrook-White equation provides the most accurate determination of the friction factor, it requires an iterative approach to determine $f$. A widely used approximate method is the Haaland Method (\ref{eq::haaland} for the transition region (2300 $\leq$ Re $\leq$ 4000) and the turbulent region (Re $\geq$ 4000). Where for a fully developed laminar flow (Re $\leq$ 2300) the friction factor can be determined using the Hagen-Poiseuille relation \ref{eq::hagenposuille} \cite{Darcyfrictionfactor}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq::haaland}
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{f}}=-1.8 \log _{10}\left[\left(\frac{(\varepsilon / D)}{3.7}\right)^{1.11}+\frac{6.9}{\operatorname{Re}}\right]
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq::hagenposuille}
    f = \frac{64}{Re}
\end{equation}

This requires an iterative approach to determine $f$. There are also non-iterative methods, but they have a relative error to the Colebrook-White Equation. Nonetheless, the authors of \cite{Darcyfrictionfactor} found that for certain correlations, the error is negligible.

With the roughness of the pipe $\epsilon$ [m].

\textbf{The Courant number (C)} can be interpreted as the distance of the advective flow per element in the space domain. It is used in the Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy (CFL) condition, which is a necessary condition to guarantee numerical stability when solving partial differential equations with an explicit approach. In the 1-dimensional case, it is defined as
\begin{equation}
    C = \frac{v \Delta t}{\Delta x} \leq 1
\end{equation}
With $v$ [m/s] as the propagation speed, $\Delta t$ [s] the simulation timestep, and $\Delta x$ [m] the discrete spatial interval \cite{MaxCourant}. 

\section{Model structure}
This section discusses the overall model structure used for the thermodynamics and hydraulics in district heating networks. In general, three different modeling techniques can be distinguished for any system:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{White-box} modeling looks at physical laws and parameters to create a model. It is the more conventional option used in most DHN modeling articles \cite{sibeijn2025economic,PipePDE}.
    \item \textbf{Black-box} modeling encapsulates the behavior of the entire system in a single function, which is learned from data. In \cite{GUELPA2016586}\cite{KECEBAS2012339}, it is applied to district heating networks.
    \item \textbf{Grey box} modeling is a combination of the two above and can be found in a DHN context in \cite{grey1}\cite{grey2}. 
\end{itemize}
This thesis project uses a white-box model approach, as Eneco prefers a more thorough understanding of the system's inner workings. 

District heating networks are branched or looped, making them well-suited for modeling using a graph approach. The graph consists of nodes, which are junctions in the system, connected by edges. An edge is a pipe, potentially equipped with a heat exchanger, pump, or valve \cite{sibeijn2025economic}. The mathematical formulation of hydraulics and thermodynamics in the graph is presented in Section \ref{sec::Graph}, since the component definitions must be introduced first. First, the hydraulic modeling of the components will be discussed before the thermodynamics is introduced. Assuming a decoupled system, the hydraulic problem is computed first, and the resulting mass flow rates are then used in the thermodynamic calculation. 

\section{Hydraulic modeling}\label{sec::hydromodel}
It is essential to know the pressure loss across all components, as this information, combined with Kirchhoff's laws, enables the determination of fluid flow throughout the district heating network. 

The total pressure loss of a pipe system can be divided into major and minor losses. The major pressure loss ($\Delta p_p$) is caused by friction along the length of the pipe and the height difference; the minor losses ($\Delta p_m$) are the result of the entrance or exit of the pipe, fittings, bends, valves, and sudden expansions and contractions of the pipes. Although they are called minor losses, a partially closed valve can cause a greater pressure loss than a long pipe \cite{white2011fluid}. The pressure loss across the valves ($\Delta p_{v}$) is therefore modeled separately in greater detail. The remaining minor losses are neglected. 

The pressure loss over the heat exchangers ($\Delta p_{\text{hex}}$) is also added to the total pressure loss within a loop of the system, resulting in the following definition:  

\begin{equation}
    \Delta p_{\text{tot}} = \sum^{N_p}_{i=1} \Delta p_{p,i} + \sum^{N_{\text{hex}}}_{j=1} \Delta p_{\text{hex},j} + \sum^{N_v}_{k=1}\Delta p_{v,k}
\end{equation}
with $N_p$ as the total number of pipes, $N_{\text{hex}}$ the total number of heat exchangers and $N_v$ the total number of valves within a loop. 

\subsection{Pipes}\label{sec::hydropipes}
An approximation of the 1-dimensional compressible Euler equations for cylindrical pipes is used to describe the dynamics within a pipe \cite{Krug2020}. The first two equations, the continuity equation and the 1D momentum equation, are needed for the calculation of the mass flows. They are stated below.

\begin{equation}
\partial_t \rho_w+\partial_x\left(\rho_w v\right)=0
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\partial_t (\rho_w v)+\partial_x(\rho_w v^2)+ \partial_x p+\rho_w g \hat{z}  +f \frac{\rho_w}{2 D}|v| v=0
\end{equation}
Where the pressure in the pipe $p$ [Pa], the slope of the pipe $\hat{z}$ [-] and friction coefficient $f$ [-]. Given the substantial difference in time scales between the hydraulics and thermodynamics, and the dominant influence of the friction term over the inertial term, the hydraulic system can be assumed to be in a steady state. In combination with the the assumptions stated in Section \ref{sec::preliminaries}, the following holds: $\partial_x v = 0, \partial_t \rho_w = 0$, $\partial_x \rho_w = 0$ and $\partial_t v = 0$. Making the continuity equation obsolete results in the approximation of the momentum equation below \cite{sibeijn2025economic}. 
\begin{equation}\label{eq::mom}
\partial_x p + \rho_w g \hat{z} +f \frac{\rho_w}{2 D}\left|v\right| v=0
\end{equation}
Substituting $v = \frac{4 \dot{q}}{\pi D^2}$ into \eqref{eq::mom} and discretizing $\partial_x p = \frac{\Delta p}{L}$ leads to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::DisPresQ}
    \Delta p_p =  f \frac{8\rho_w L}{\pi^2 D^5}\left|q\right| q + \rho_w g L \hat{z}
\end{equation}
With the flow rate $q$ [m$^3$/s] and the length of the pipe $L$ [m]. The first term on the right side of the equation is the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which describes the pressure loss due to friction between the fluid and the pipe wall. Throughout this project, the mass flow rate, $\dot{m}$ [kg/s], is used in place of the volumetric flow rate. Therefore, (\ref{eq::DisPresQ}) becomes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::DisPresQ_mflow}
\Delta p_p = f\frac{8 L}{\pi^2 D^5 \rho_w} |\dot{m}|\dot{m} + \rho_w g L \hat{z}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Valves}\label{sec::valves}
Several types of valves are present in the Decentralized Solutions. This project focuses on the 2-way control valves in the heat exchangers and the thermostatic bypass valve situated at the top of the risers. The following equation can express the pressure loss across a valve \cite{Artikelphdchris}:

\begin{equation}\label{eq::valvedp}
    \Delta p_{v} = \left( \frac{\dot{m}}{K_v} \right)^{2} \frac{\rho_w}{\rho_{\text{ref}}}   =   \left( \frac{\dot{m}}{K_v} \right)^{2}
\end{equation}
With the hydraulic conductivity parameter $K_v$  [kg/s/$\sqrt{Pa}$], the actual fluid density during simulations $\rho$ [kg/m$^3$], and the fluid density during the manufacturer's $K_v$ measurements $\rho_{ref}$ (typically 1000 kg/m$^3$). As the density of the water is assumed to be constant, you end up with the right-hand term. 

The calculation of the valve characteristic $K_v$, which varies with the valve's opening position, depends on the valve type. For small valve lifts, the $K_v$ value cannot be determined accurately because it exceeds the required tolerances for the valve's basic shape. The valve's control range is determined up to a 10 percent deviation of the basic shape. When it goes outside of this control range, the valve becomes unpredictable \cite{echtephdthesis}. In general, the manufacturer provides the control range. 

\subsubsection{2-Way Control Valves}
The 2-way control valves focus on controlling the flow. When this valve is a basic linear 2-way valve, $K_v$ can be determined using (\ref{eq::linear2valve}). Where (\ref{eq::equalpercentvalve}) applies to an equal percentage valve. Both equations are in accordance with the VDI/VDE2173 standard \cite{VDI/VDE2713}. 

\begin{equation}\label{eq::linear2valve}
    \frac{K_v}{K_{vs}} = \left( 1 -\frac{K_{v,0}}{K_{vs}} \right) h + \frac{K_{v,0}}{K_{vs}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq::equalpercentvalve}
    \frac{K_v}{K_{vs}} = {\left(\frac{K_{vs}}{K_{v,0}} \right)}^{h - 1}
\end{equation}

With the $K_v$ value for the completely opened valve as $K_{vs}$, the $K_v$ value at the points where the basic shape of the valve characteristic intersects with the y-axis as $K_{v,0}$, and the dimensionless valve displacement $h$, which ranges from 0 to 1 \cite{Artikelphdchris}. In HVAC applications, the $K_{vs}/K_{v,0}$ ratio ranges from 25 to 50. However, in practical applications, the valve is not ideal. The actual pressure difference across it may deviate from the setpoint. Especially for small valve lifts, the shape of the actual $K_v$ value cannot be accurately defined anymore. Therefore, $K_{vr}$ is defined as the minimum $K_v$ value for which the deviation from the reference curve remains within 10\% and the $h_{\text{lift}}$ associated with it is called $h^{*}$. Below this threshold, the valve operates outside its controllable range, and its behavior becomes unreliable. This thesis models in accordance with the authors from \cite{echtephdthesis} this region with a linear relation for every valve characteristic type, shown in Figure \ref{fig::real_Kv}  If the valve is closed, the leakage coefficient $K_{\text{v,leak}}$ is defined, the ratio $K_{\text{vs}}/K_{\text{v,leak}}$ ranges from 2000 to 5000 in hydronic applications \cite{echtephdthesis}.

No data was available on the valve characteristics of the components considered in this project. Therefore, assumptions are made based on common practice. The thermostatic bypass valve is assumed to be linear because this yields a proportional relationship between $K_v$ and the valve lift $h(t)$ over the full operating range. The valves in the heat exchangers are assumed to be equal percentage valves, as the flow variations are larger, and this characteristic provides better controllability over a wider operating range. The valve characteristic of both types is shown in Figure \ref{fig::ideal_Kv}

\subsection{Heat Exchangers}\label{sec:::hydroHEX}
To accurately capture the pressure drop across the heat exchanger body, the individual pressure losses associated with the plates and ports can be modeled. Where the pressure loss over the plates depends on the Reynolds number \cite{FemkeJanssenLit}. However, as the information required for such a detailed approach is unavailable, a simplified pressure-drop model is adopted. The pressure drop is assumed to be proportional to the square of the mass flow rate, consistent with more detailed pressure-drop correlations, while neglecting the effect of the Reynolds number.

\begin{equation}
    \Delta p_{\text{hex}} = K_p |\dot{m}| \dot{m}
\end{equation}
with $K_p$ [1/(kg m)] as the pressure loss coefficient of the heat exchanger.

In addition to the pressure loss caused by the heat exchanger body, it also contains a flow control valve. Within the Arctic WKW-S 4P, several settings are available for how the valve displacement should respond to the consumer's heat demand. As modeling these settings was deemed outside the scope of this project, it was decided to control the valve with a PI controller. This controller starts operating once there is mass flow on the consumer side of the heat exchanger and aims to raise this water to 55 $^{\circ}C$. The PI-controller is equipped with anti-windup to prevent the integrator from accumulating error when the valve is saturated. The modeling of the consumer side behavior of the heat exchanger is further discussed in Section \ref{sec::heatdemand}.

\subsection{Pumps}
Pumps supply the pressure necessary for the fluid to flow through the network. Centrifugal pumps are the most commonly used pumps in district heating networks. The pump is characterized by its performance curve, as shown in Figure \ref{fig::pumpcharacteristic}, which illustrates the relationship between pump head (or pressure) and the volumetric flow rate. This figure includes curves for various rotational shaft speeds. The system head curve is also plotted, with the system's operating points located at the intersections of the pump and system curves. The figure further includes iso-efficiency lines, which represent constant pump efficiency across different operating conditions. Changes in valve positions alter the system head curve, thereby shifting the operating point. Combining the valve positions with the shaft rotational speed enables efficient pump operation. Pump curves are modeled using a polynomial law \cite{echtephdthesis}. The pump curves in the developed simulation model are represented by second-order polynomials, as this was deemed sufficient to reproduce a valid system curve.

\begin{equation}\label{eq::pumpchar}  
    \Delta p_{p} = \sum^{\text{n}}_{\text{i=1}} \left( c_i(\omega)  \dot{m}^{i} \right)
\end{equation}

With the rotational shaft speed $\omega$ and the polynomial constants $c_i(\omega)$. The pump characteristics in the developed simulation model are approximated by a second-order polynomial, which was found to be sufficient for accurately reproducing the system curve and implemented by Sandou et al. as well \cite{Sandou2005}. The pump is assumed to operate at a constant rotational shaft speed, such that its operating point remains on a single pump curve. Leading to the following relation of the delivered pump pressure:

\begin{equation}\label{eq::pump2order}
    \Delta p_{p} = c_2 \dot{m}^{2} + c_1 \dot{m} + c_3
\end{equation}
Where $c_1$, $c_2$, and $c_3$ are the polynomial constants.

\section{Thermo-Dynamic modeling}\label{sec::thermo}
Once the hydraulics calculation is performed, the resulting mass flows will be used for the thermodynamic system. As the fractional heat losses are assumed to be negligible (see Section \ref{sec::preliminaries}), the thermodynamic model of the Decentralized Solutions consists of two main components: the pipelines and the heat exchangers. These components are discussed in more detail below. 

\subsection{Pipes}\label{sec::thermopipes}
The thermodynamic behavior of the water within a pipeline is modeled using the fluid's energy balance in the one-dimensional PDE pipe model given by (\ref{eq::pipePDE}).

\begin{equation}\label{eq::pipePDE}
\underbrace{\rho_w c_{p,w} A \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}}_1+\underbrace{\dot{m} c_{p,w} \frac{\partial T}{\partial x}}_2=\underbrace{\kappa_w A \frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2}}_3+\underbrace{\frac{\left(T_a-T\right)}{R'}}_4
\end{equation}

With the specific heat of water $c_{p,w}$ [kJ / (kg K)], the cross-sectional area of the pipe A [m$^2$], the temperature of the water T [K], the thermal conductivity of water $\kappa_w$ [W / (m K)], the ambient temperature $T_a$ [K], and the total thermal resistance per unit length of the pipe $R'$ [(m K) / W]. The total heat transmission coefficient $k_h$ can also be used instead of $R'$, as $k_h = \frac{1}{R'}$. 
In (\ref{eq::pipePDE}), the first term represents the change in internal energy, the second term accounts for the enthalpy flux of the water, the third represents conductive heat transfer within the water, and the last term describes convective heat transfer from the fluid to its surroundings.

Applying assumptions 5 and 10 from Section  \ref{sec::preliminaries} simplifies the PDE into: 
 
\begin{equation}\label{eq::simpipePDE}
\rho_w c_{p,w} A \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} + \dot{m} c_{p,w} \frac{\partial T}{\partial x}=\frac{\left(T_a-T\right)}{R'}
\end{equation}
The total thermal resistance per unit length of the pipe is determined as: 

\begin{equation}
R^{\prime}=\frac{\ln \left(\frac{r_b}{r_a}\right)}{2 \pi k_{a b}}+\frac{\ln \left(\frac{r_c}{r_b}\right)}{2 \pi k_{b c}}+\frac{1}{2 \pi r_{c} \bar{h} }
\end{equation}

% \frac{1}{S k_s}
% +\frac{\ln \left(\frac{r_d}{r_c}\right)}{2 \pi k_{c d}}

With the thermal conductivities of the pipe and the insulation $k_{ab}, k_{bc}$ [W/mK], the radii are depicted in Figure \ref{fig::pipe} and the average convection heat transfer coefficient from the pipe to the air $\bar{h}$ [W/m$^2$K]. The convective heat transfer between the fluid and the pipe is neglected as it is relatively small compared to the other thermal resistances \cite{booklowT}.

\subsubsection{Node Method}\label{sec::NodeMethod}
The Node Method (NM) introduces a discretized solution to the thermodynamic PDE, in which temperatures are calculated only at the pipeline outlet, using the inlet temperatures from previous time steps. This discretized form follows from transforming \eqref{eq::simpipePDE} into a new reduced set of coordinates that describe the solution, using the Method of Characteristics. The final formula for the outlet temperature of the pipeline $T_{\text{out,2}}$ at time step $k$ is presented below:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::NMfinaleq}
    T_{\mathrm{out},2}(k) = T_a(k) + \left(T_{\mathrm{out},1,\mathrm{st}}(k)
    - T_a(k)\right)\exp\!\left(-\frac{t_{\mathrm{stay}}(k)}{R'\rho_w c_{p,w} A}\right)
\end{equation}
with $T_a$ [$^{\circ}$C] the ambient temperature, $T_{\mathrm{out},1,\mathrm{st}}$ [$^{\circ}$C] the steel-corrected lossless output temperature defined in \eqref{eq::Theatcapacity}, and $t_{\text{stay}}$ [s] the average length of stay of the water masses leaving the pipeline, described in \eqref{eq::averagestay}.

\begin{subequations}\label{eq::NodeMethod}
\centering
\begin{align} 
T_{\mathrm{out}}(k) &= \frac{1}{\dot{m}(k)\Delta k}
    \Bigl[\bigl(R(k) - \rho_w A L\bigr)\,T_{\mathrm{in}}(k-\gamma(k)) \notag \\
&\quad + \sum_{\kappa=k-\varepsilon(k)+1}^{k-\gamma(k)-1}
    \!\bigl(\dot{m}(\kappa)\Delta k\,T_{\mathrm{in}}(\kappa)\bigr)
  + \bigl(\dot{m}(k)\Delta k + \rho_w A L - S(k)\bigr)\,
    T_{\mathrm{in}}(k-\varepsilon(k))\Bigr], \label{eq::NMTout} \\[8pt]
\gamma(k) &= \min_{n} \left\{ n \geq 0,\; n \in \mathbb{Z} : 
    \sum_{i=k-n}^{k} \dot{m}(i)\,\Delta k > \rho_w A L \right\}, 
    \label{eq::gammadelay} \\[4pt]
\varepsilon(k) &= \min_{n} \left\{ n \geq 0,\; n \in \mathbb{Z} : 
    \sum_{i=k-n}^{k} \dot{m}(i)\,\Delta k > \rho_w A L + \dot{m}(k)\,\Delta k \right\}.
    \label{eq::epsilondelay}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

Equation~\eqref{eq::NMTout} gives the lossless outlet temperature of the pipeline $T_{\mathrm{out,1}}$ by performing a weighted average of the temperature of the respective water masses passing the outlet, where no thermal losses are yet taken into account. The auxiliary variables $\gamma$  and $\varepsilon$ denote the time steps at which the water mass entered the pipeline (edge), which are now the last ($\gamma$) and respectively first ($\varepsilon$) to leave the pipeline during timestep $k$. Using these variables, the auxiliary water masses $R$ and $S$ can be calculated, as illustrated by Figure~\ref{fig::NodeMethod}.

t_{\mathrm{stay}}(k) = {} & \frac{1}{\dot{m}(k)\Delta k}

    \!\bigl((k-\kappa)\,\dot{m}(\kappa)\Delta k\bigr)

\begin{equation}\label{eq::Theatcapacity}
    T_{\mathrm{out},1,\mathrm{st}}(k)
    = \frac{T_{\mathrm{out},1}(k)\,\dot{m}(k)\,c_{p,w}\,\Delta k
            + C_{\mathrm{st}}\,T_{\mathrm{st}}(k-1)}%

\end{equation}
with $T_{\mathrm{st}}(k-1)$ the steel core temperature at the previous time step, updated as $T_{\mathrm{st}}(k) = T_{\mathrm{out},1,\mathrm{st}}(k)$. Here

\begin{equation}

    \rho_{\mathrm{st}}\,c_{p,\mathrm{st}}\,L,
\end{equation}
with $d_1$ and $d_2$ [m] the pipeline's inner and outer diameters,
$c_{p,\mathrm{st}}$ [kJ/(kg\,K)] the specific heat capacity and
$\rho_{\mathrm{st}}$ [kg/m$^3$] the density of the steel

\subsection{Heat Exchanger}
Heat exchangers exchange thermal energy between the district heating network and the domestic heating system. It was chosen to model the Arctic WKW-S 4P \cite{fortes_wkw_s_4p}, a plate heat exchanger used in several Eneco DCS projects. It consists of a series of corrugated plates mounted in a frame. Separated by the plates, the hot and cold fluids flow counter-currently, exchanging heat through the thin plate material. Although a dynamic heat exchanger model can be developed in great detail using the Finite Volume Method (FVM), insufficient information is available about the system's internal layout. Therefore, this thesis does not model the heat exchanger's internal temperature profile and focuses solely on heat transfer between the hot and cold fluids. It is assumed that the rate of heat transfer from the hot fluid ($\dot{Q_{h}}$ [J / s]) is equal to the rate of heat transfer from the cold fluid ($\dot{Q_{c}}$). That means that we do not lose thermal energy. 

\begin{align}
    \dot{Q_h} &= \dot{m}_hc_h(T_{h,out} - T_{h,in}) = C_h (T_{h,out} - T_{h,in}) \\  
    \dot{Q_c} &= \dot{m}_cc_c(T_{c,out} - T_{c,in}) = C_c (T_{c,out} - T_{c,in})
\end{align}

With the mass flow rates of the hot and cold fluids denoted by \( \dot{m}_h \) and \( \dot{m}_c \) [kg/s], and the specific heat capacities by \( c_h \) and \( c_c \) [J/(kg K)], the inlet and outlet temperatures of the hot fluid are given as \( T_{h,\text{in}} \) and \( T_{h,\text{out}} \) [\( ^\circ \)C], and those of the cold fluid as \( T_{c,\text{in}} \) and \( T_{c,\text{out}} \) [\( ^\circ \)C], respectively. The corresponding heat capacity rates are \( C_h \) and \( C_c \) [J/(K s)] for the hot and cold fluids. 

\subsubsection{Effectiveness-NTU method}
The Effectiveness-NTU ($\epsilon$-NTU) is based on a steady-state formulation. Consequently, it is assumed that the mass flow rates and inlet temperatures remain constant during each time step, and that the fluid entering the heat exchanger passes through it completely within that time step. In addition, the heat transfer coefficient is assumed to be uniform along the entire heat exchanger. To maintain compatibility with the CFD model, a time step of 1 s is used. Such a small time step challenges the steady-state assumptions, as temperatures and mass flow rates may vary rapidly over these short intervals. However, the objective of this study is to simulate the operation of multiple heat exchangers over an entire day, over which the effects of short-term fluctuations are expected to be partially averaged out. Therefore, given both the scope of the study and the lack of detailed information regarding the internal configuration of the heat exchangers, the steady-state approach is considered acceptable for the present work. Nevertheless, the validity of this assumption should be assessed in future research.

The method revolves around the dimensionless parameter $\epsilon$, the thermodynamic efficiency. 

\begin{equation}\label{eq::epsilon}
    \epsilon = \frac{\dot{Q}}{\dot{Q}_{max}} = \frac{\dot{Q}}{{C}_{min}(T_{h,in} - T_{c,in})}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    C_{min} = \min(C_h, C_c)
\end{equation}
With the thermodynamic maximum achievable power $\dot{Q}_{max}$ [J/s], and the minimum heat capacity rate $C_{min}$ [J/ (K s)]. After performing an energy balance, these values are combined to calculate the heat exchanger effectiveness \cite{cengel2025heat}.
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon = \frac{1 - e^{-NTU  (1 - C^*)}}{1 - C^*  e^{-NTU (1 - C^*)}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    NTU = \frac{U A_s}{C_{min}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    C^{*} = \frac{C_{\min}}{C_{\max}}
\end{equation}
With the overall heat transfer coefficient of the heat exchanger $U$ [J / ($m^2$ K  s)] and the heat transfer surface area $A_{s}$ [m$^2$].

\subsubsection{Logarithmic Mean Temperature Difference}
The Logarithmic Mean Temperature Difference (LMTD) is used to determine the overall heat transfer coefficient and the heat transfer surface area, the derivation of the physical constants can be found in Chapter \ref{chap::constants}. Figure \ref{fig::counterhex} shows a basic counter-flow heat exchanger. When there is a difference in heat capacity rates, the temperature difference along the heat exchanger is not consistent, illustrated in Figure \ref{fig::counterflowHEX}. This requires the use of a mean temperature difference ($\Delta T_m$) to be able to use Newton's law of cooling, Equation \ref{eq::newtoncooling}. The Logarithmic Mean Temperature Difference ($\Delta_{LM}$) is developed for this case. However, the formula depicted in \eqref{eq::LMTD} is determined for the basic counter-flow heat exchanger from Figure \ref{fig::counterhex}, where the plate heat exchanger uses a multi-pass arrangement. To account for this design difference, a correction factor ($F$) is typically applied, with a value of around 0.95 for plate heat exchangers \cite{FemkeJanssenLit}. This factor depends on the flow arrangement and the number of transform units (NTU). 

\begin{equation}\label{eq::newtoncooling}
    \dot{Q} =  U A_{s} \Delta T_{m}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \Delta T_m = F \Delta T_{LM}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq::LMTD}
\Delta T_{\operatorname{lm}}=\frac{(T_{h,in} - T_{c,out}) - (T_{h,out} - T_{c,in})}{\ln \left(\frac{T_{h,in} - T_{c,out}}{T_{h,out} - T_{c,in}}\right)}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Heat transfer coefficient}
To obtain a more physically realistic heat transfer model, particularly to compensate for the overestimation of heat transfer at low flow rates, the overall heat transfer coefficient is modeled as a function of the mass flow rate. In the $\epsilon$-NTU method, the thermal conductance of the heat exchanger wall material is neglected, so the overall heat transfer is determined solely by the convective heat transfer between the hot and cold fluids. Resulting in the following relation for the thermal conductance ($UA_s$ [W/K]).

\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{UA_s} = \frac{1}{hA_h} + \frac{1}{hA_c}
    \label{eq:UA_series}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $hA_h$ and $hA_c$ are the convective thermal conductances of the hot and cold side, respectively. The convective heat transfer coefficient $h$ is related to the Nusselt number through, see \ref{eq::Nusselt}. For turbulent flow, the Nusselt number is described by the Dittus-Boelter equation:

\begin{equation}
    Nu = 0.023 \cdot Re^{0.8} \cdot Pr^{n}
    \label{eq:dittus_boelter}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $n = 0.4$ for heating and $n = 0.3$ for cooling. Since $Re \propto \dot{m}$, and all fluid and geometry properties can be collected into a constant, it follows directly that:

\begin{equation}
    h \propto \dot{m}^{0.8}
    \label{eq:h_scaling}
\end{equation}

The same approach within the Modelica Buildings library in the \texttt{PlateHeatExchangerEffectivenessNTU} module is applied, which uses the proportional relation in their analogy to couple the mass flow to the thermal conductance by scaling around the heat exchanger's design point \cite{BuildingsLibrary}:

\begin{equation}
    hA(\dot{m}) = hA_{\text{des}} \left(\frac{\dot{m}}{\dot{m}_{\text{des}}}\right)^{0.8}
    \label{eq:hA_scaling}
\end{equation}

\noindent Assuming the nominal conductance $UA_{des} = U \cdot A_s$ is equally split over both sides, $hA_{h,des} = hA_{c,des} = 2\,UA_{des}$, and substituting into \eqref{eq:UA_series}:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{UA_s} = 
    \frac{1}{2\,UA_{\text{des}}} \left(\frac{\dot{m}_h}{\dot{m}_{\text{h,des}}}\right)^{-0.8}
    + \frac{1}{2\,UA_{\text{des}}} \left(\frac{\dot{m}_c}{\dot{m}_{\text{c,des}}}\right)^{-0.8}
    \label{eq:UA_offdesign}
\end{equation}

\section{Heat supply}
Eneco's Decentralized Solutions prioritize the use of the ATES system for heating and cooling due to its sustainable operation. Depending on the system configuration, the season, and whether the heating or cooling demand is at peak load, the system can operate in several different modes. This study focuses on periods of low heating demand, which primarily occur in summer, regardless of whether demand is at peak conditions. During these periods, the ATES mode supplies the required heat through its backup heating network. This backup network can obtain heat from various sources, including gas-fired boilers, excess heat from power plants, and waste processing facilities \cite{heatsupply}. The dynamics of the heat supply system are beyond the scope of this thesis and are therefore not explicitly modeled. The heat supply will be modeled as a supply temperature, which can be specified upfront or determined by the optimization scheme.

\section{Graph}\label{sec::Graph}
% District heating networks are branched or looped, making them well-suited for modeling using a graph approach. The graph consists of nodes, which are junctions in the system, connected by edges. An edge is a pipe, potentially equipped with a heat exchanger, pump, or valve \cite{sibeijn2025economic}. On the edges, the fluid loses energy to its surroundings, the producers can add heat, and consumers can subtract it \todo{dit klopt niet helemaal, maar weet even niet hoe ik het anders op moet schrijven}. The temperature at each node is determined by a weighted average of the incoming mass flows and their respective temperatures. All outgoing edges from the node are assigned the same temperature as the node itself. To calculate the mass flows and pressure within the network, Kirchhoff's two laws must be upheld. The first law asserts that the total mass flow entering and leaving a node must be equal, ensuring mass conservation. The second law requires that the net pressure drop around any closed loop in the network must be zero, maintaining pressure balance. The pressure loss within the pipes depends quadratically on the flow, making the hydraulics a system of non-linear equations. As we assume a decoupled system, the hydraulic problem is computed first, after which the obtained mass flow rates are used for the thermodynamic system. The thermodynamic behavior of the edge in the system is described by a PDE. Different solutions for this equation are discussed in more detail in Section \ref{sec::thermo}. The mathematical formulation of the graph is presented in Section \ref{sec::graph}, as first the component definitions need to be introduced. 

The overall model structure of the district heating network was decided to be a strongly connected directed graph. The looped and branched nature of district heating networks makes them well-suited for a graph, which is commonly used in the literature \cite{sibeijn2025economic, Krug2020,OPPELT2016336,Simonssongraph}. The strong connectedness indicates that no water leaves the system. The mathematical definition of the graph is largely inspired by Sibeijn et al. \cite{sibeijn2025economic}.

The graph is defined as $\mathcal{G}=(\mathcal{N}, 
\mathcal{E})$, with $\mathcal{N} := \{1,2,...,|\mathcal{N}|\}$ the set of nodes representing the volume-less junctions in the network, and 
$\mathcal{E} := \{1,2,...,|\mathcal{E}|\}$ the set of edges, where 
$\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{N} \times \mathcal{N}$. The node-to-node 
connectivity is described by the adjacency matrix $D \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{N}|\times |\mathcal{N}|}$, where for any $(i,j) \in \mathcal{N} \times \mathcal{N}$:

\begin{equation}\label{eq::adjacencymatrix}
D_{ij}= \begin{cases}
1, & \text{if } (i,j) \in \mathcal{E}, \\
0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The edge-to-node relationship is captured by the incidence matrix 
$\mathbf{A} \in \{-1,0,1\}^{|\mathcal{N}| \times |\mathcal{E}|}$, where for 
any $(i,k) \in \mathcal{N} \times \mathcal{E}$:

\begin{equation}\label{eq::incidencematrix}
A_{ik}= \begin{cases}
-1, & \text{if the } k^{\text{th}} \text{ edge exits node } i, \\
 1, & \text{if the } k^{\text{th}} \text{ edge enters node } i, \\
 0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The loop matrix $\mathbf{L} \in \{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{L}| \times |\mathcal{E}|}$ 
describes which edges belong to which independent loop, with $|\mathcal{L}|$ 
the number of independent loops given by the cycle rank $|\mathcal{L}| = |\mathcal{E}| - |\mathcal{N}| + 1$. For any 
$(j,k) \in \mathcal{L} \times \mathcal{E}$:

\begin{equation}\label{eq::loopmatrix}
L_{jk}=\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if pipe } k \text{ belongs to loop } j, \\
0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\noindent Since the flow direction is assumed to be fixed, all edges belonging 
to a loop are assigned a value of $1$.

\subsection{Edges}
An edge is a pipe, potentially equipped with a pump, valve, or the pressure drop over the heat exchanger body. The dynamics of an edge ($e \in \mathcal{E}$) can be described with the two PDEs, derived in Sections \ref{sec::hydropipes} and \ref{sec::thermopipes}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq::formom}
\partial_x p_e + \rho_w g \hat{z} + f\frac{8 L}{\pi^2 D^5 \rho_w} |\dot{m}_e|\dot{m}_e =0
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq::forsimpipePDE}
\rho_w c_{p,w} A \frac{\partial T_e}{\partial t} + \dot{m}_e c_{p,w} \frac{\partial T_e}{\partial x}=\frac{\left(T_a-T_e\right)}{R'} + w_e
\end{equation}
The hydraulic relation can be discretized with $\partial_x p = \frac{\Delta p}{L}$ which leads to
\begin{equation}\label{eq::thermopde}
    \Delta p_e = f\frac{8 L}{\pi^2 D^5 \rho_w} |\dot{m}_e|\dot{m}_e + \rho_w g L \hat{z}.
\end{equation}
When a pump is connected to an edge, the pressure loss over the edge becomes
% \begin{equation}
%     \Delta p_e = R_{f}\,\dot{m_e}^2 + \rho_w g L \hat{z} - h_e(r_e) 
% \end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \Delta p_e = f\frac{8 L}{\pi^2 D^5 \rho_w} |\dot{m}_e|\dot{m}_e + \rho_w g L \hat{z} - (c_2 \dot{m}_e^{2} + c_1 \dot{m}_e + c_3)
\end{equation}
Additional pressure loss of other components can be added following their definitions in Section \ref{sec::hydromodel}, but one needs to take into account that the pressure loss will be added, not subtracted, as with the pump.

The second Kirchhoff law states that the net pressure difference over a closed loop must be zero. A set of edges connected in a closed loop is defined as $\mathcal{E}_{\text{loop}} = \left\{e \in \mathcal{E}: e \ \text{in closed loop}\right\}$, formulating the relation as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::secondKirchhoff}
    \sum_{e  \in \mathcal{E}_{\text{loop}}} \Delta p_e = 0.
\end{equation}

The solution of the PDE \eqref{eq::thermopde} is calculated using the Node Method, resulting in the following equation; its derivation can be found in Section \ref{sec::NodeMethod}. 

\begin{equation}
    T(L,k) = T_a(k) + \left(T_{\mathrm{out},1,\mathrm{st}}(k)
    - T_a(k)\right)\exp\!\left(-\frac{t_{\mathrm{stay}}(k)}{R'\rho_w c_{p,w} A}\right)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Nodes}
The first Kirchhoff law states that the sum of flows entering a node equals the sum of flows exiting the node. This results in the conservation of mass over every node due to the assumed incompressibility of the water. For any node $n \in \mathcal{N}$, the edge sets $\mathcal{E}_{\rightarrow n} = \left\{ e \in \mathcal{E} : e \  \text{enters} \ n\right\}$ and $\mathcal{E}_{n\rightarrow } = \left\{ e \in \mathcal{E} : e \  \text{exits} \ n\right\}$ are respectively the entering and exiting sets of edges. The mass conservation is given by

\begin{equation}
    \sum_{e  \in \mathcal{E}_{\rightarrow n}} \dot{m}_e(t) = \sum_{e  \in \mathcal{E}_{n \rightarrow}} \dot{m}_e(t).
\end{equation}

Since frictional heat is neglected and heat capacity is assumed constant, the energy balance in every node can be used to define a mixing rule for the node's temperature based on the incoming water temperatures. However, the nature of the Node Method does not allow heat transfer from the heat exchanger to occur at an edge; it must occur at a node. Therefore, the heat exchanger is modeled as two edges connected by a node. While the pressure drop caused by the heat exchanger body and its control valve is modeled on the incoming edge, the heat transfer occurs within the node using the $\epsilon$-NTU method. Therefore the set of heat exchanger nodes is separately defined $\mathcal{N_{\text{hex}}}$ with $\mathcal{N_{\text{hex}}} := \{1,2, ..., |\mathcal{N_{\text{hex}}}|\}$. For every $n \in \mathcal{N_{\text{hex}}}$ the nodal temperature $T_n(t)$ is defined as:

\begin{align}
    T_n(t) &= f_{\epsilon-\text{NTU}}(T_e(t),Q_{d,i}(t)), \quad \forall n \in  \mathcal{N_{\text{hex}}}, \ e  \in \mathcal{E}_{\rightarrow n} \label{eq::NTUnodes} \\
    T_n(t) &= \frac{\sum_{e  \in \mathcal{E}_{\rightarrow n}} \dot{m}_e(t) T_e(t)}{\sum_{e  \in \mathcal{E}_{\rightarrow n}} \dot{m}_e(t)}, \quad \forall n \in \mathcal{N} \setminus \mathcal{N}_{\text{hex}} \label{eq::TempMix}
\end{align}

with the temperature at the nodal temperature being the temperature of all the exiting edges \cite{Krug2020}. 
\begin{equation}
    T_n(t) = T_e(t), \quad \forall \ n \in \mathcal{N}, e \in \mathcal{E}_{n \rightarrow} 
\end{equation}

\subsection{Solving Kirchhoff's laws}
% \todo[inline]{ingaan op hoe het netwerk van non-linear equations eruit ziet dus met matrix en shit}
The use of the Kirchhoff laws yields a system of nonlinear equations, which are set equal to zero to obtain the mass flow rates throughout the system at timestep $k$.

\begin{equation}
F(\dot{\mathbf{m}}(k)) = 
\begin{bmatrix}
    A_{\text{red}}\, \dot{\mathbf{m}}(k) \\[6pt]
    L \, \mathbf{\Delta p_e} (\dot{\mathbf{m}}(k))
\end{bmatrix}
= \mathbf{0},
\end{equation}
where $F(\dot{\mathbf{m}}(k)) \in \mathbb{R}^{(|\mathcal{L}|+|\mathcal{N}|-1)}$ is composed of two blocks: the nodal mass balance residuals $A_{\text{red}}\,\dot{\mathbf{m}}(k) \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{N}|-1}$, one equation per node excluding the reference node, and the pressure drop residuals $L \, \mathbf{\Delta p_e} (\dot{\mathbf{m}}(k)) \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{L}|}$, one equation per fundamental loop, with the vector $\dot{\mathbf{m}}(k) \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{E}|}$ containing all the mass flow rates of the edges at timestep $k$,

The Hardy-Cross \cite{HardyCross} and Newton-Raphson \cite{NewtonenHard} methods are often used to solve such systems \cite{KUNTUAROVA}. These iterative methods start with an initial guess of the flow and adjust it iteratively until it converges. While the Hardy-Cross method iterates through each loop independently, the Newton–Raphson method iterates through all loops simultaneously \cite{NewtonenHard}. As a result, Newton-Raphson generally converges more quickly than Hardy-Cross. While there are several articles claiming a faster convergence or more accurate determination of the mass flows in cases with low flow rates \cite{STEVANOVIC, ARSENE}, Newton-Raphson was selected for this work, given the use of Newton-Raphson type solvers by popular simulation tools such as Pandapipes \cite{pandapipes} and OpenModelica \cite{OpenModelica} and its widely available documentation.
