\section{Bayesian Optimization overview}
Bayesian Optimization (BO) is used to find the extrema of objective functions 
that are expensive or time-consuming to evaluate. It can be applied in situations 
where there is no closed-form expression of the objective function, but it is 
possible to gather some data points of the function. BO techniques are highly 
efficient considering the number of samples needed to find an extreme. This is 
largely due to incorporating a prior belief about the problem, helping data 
sampling, and maintaining a balance while sampling the search space between 
exploration (sampling $x$ at high uncertainty of the objective function) and 
exploitation (sampling $x$ where the objective function is likely to be high). 
The technique revolves around Bayes' Theorem, stated below, hence its name.

\begin{equation}
    P(f \mid \boldsymbol{X},f(\boldsymbol{X})) \propto P(\boldsymbol{X},f(\boldsymbol{X})\mid f)\, P(f).
\end{equation}

\noindent With $x_i$ as the $i$th sample, $f(x_i)$ as the observation of the 
objective function at $x_i$, and $X = \{x_1, \ldots x_n\}$ as the sampling points samples and $f(\boldsymbol{X}) = \{f(x_1), \ldots, f(x_n)\}$ the set of its observations of the performed experiments. The prior $P(f)$ is combined with the likelihood function $P(\boldsymbol{X},f(\boldsymbol{X})\mid f)$, resulting in the posterior 
$P(f \mid\boldsymbol{X},f(\boldsymbol{X}))$, capturing the information we have of the objective function. Bayesian Optimization generally uses Gaussian Processes (GP) as the prior of the objective function. The newly found observation is then incorporated into the posterior, refining our belief about the objective function. This process is iterated until an optimum is found, with each subsequent sample location $x_{n+1}$ determined using the acquisition function. 

Figure \ref{fig::BO} shows three iterative steps of the BO technique. On the x-axis, the optimized feature is plotted, and on the y-axis, its performance is plotted. The black dotted line is the objective function, and the black line is the posterior mean. Around the posterior mean, the light purple region shows the upper and lower bounds of the posterior uncertainty. At each step, the next sampling point is chosen based on the maximum of the acquisition function. This information is then used to update the posterior, as can be clearly observed from the changes in its uncertainty. The following sections discuss Gaussian Processes and the applied acquisition function in more detail.

\subsection{Gaussian Processes}\label{sec::GP}
A Gaussian Process is a distribution over functions, completely specified by its mean function $\mu(x)$ and covariance function $k(x,x')$ for any pair of input points $x, x' \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$:

\begin{align}
    f(x) &\sim \mathcal{GP}(\mu(x),\, k(x,x')), \\
    \mu(x)  &= \mathbb{E}[f(x)], \\
    k(x,x') &= \mathbb{E}\!\left[(f(x)-\mu(x))(f(x')-\mu(x'))\right].
\end{align}

\noindent Assuming we already have the observations $\boldsymbol{X},f(\boldsymbol{X})$ and a new sampling point $x_{n+1}$ has been determined applying Bayesian Optimization, then following the properties of Gaussian processes the joint distribution over their function evaluations 
$f(\mathbf{X})$ and $f(x_{n+1})$ is:

\begin{equation}\label{eq::GP_joint}
\begin{bmatrix}
f(\mathbf{X}) \\[4pt]
f(x_{n+1})
\end{bmatrix}
\sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(
\begin{bmatrix}
\mu(\mathbf{X}) \\[4pt]
\mu(x_{n+1})
\end{bmatrix},
\begin{bmatrix}
k(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{X}) & k(\mathbf{X}, x_{n+1}) \\[4pt]
k(x_{n+1}, \mathbf{X}) & k(x_{n+1}, x_{n+1})
\end{bmatrix}
\right).
\end{equation}

\noindent The posterior distribution of $f$ at the query point, denoted 
$\tilde{f}(x_{n+1}) \sim p(f(x_{n+1})\mid\boldsymbol{X},f(\boldsymbol{X}))$, 
follows from standard Gaussian conditioning rules:

\begin{equation}\label{eq::GP_posterior}
\begin{aligned}
\tilde{f}(x_{n+1}) &\sim 
    \mathcal{N}\!\left(\tilde{\mu}_{f}(x_{n+1}),\, 
    \tilde{\Sigma}_{f}^2(x_{n+1})\right), \quad \text{where:} \\[6pt]
\tilde{\mu}_{f}(x_{n+1}) &= \mu(x_{n+1}) 
    + k(x_{n+1}, \mathbf{X})\,k(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{X})^{-1}
    \bigl(f(\mathbf{X}) - \mu(\mathbf{X})\bigr), \\[4pt]
\tilde{\Sigma}_{f}^2(x_{n+1}) &= k(x_{n+1}, x_{n+1}) 
    - k(x_{n+1}, \mathbf{X})\,k(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{X})^{-1}\,
    k(\mathbf{X}, x_{n+1}).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Acquisition function}\label{sec::acqfunc}
Acquisition functions are used to guide the search for the desired optimum of the objective function. These functions are defined such that they yield high values for potentially high (or low, depending on the extreme of interest) values of the objective function. This can be due to a high predicted objective function value, large uncertainty of this value, or a combination of both. The next sample location is obtained by maximizing the acquisition function. A proper balance between exploration and exploitation in this step is essential for BO, as it enables the method to efficiently scan the search space for potential extrema \cite{bo_tutorial}.

A popular choice, and the one adopted in this project, is the Expected Improvement (EI) of a candidate point, introduced by Mo\v{c}kus et al. \cite{Mockus1978}. Any further investigation into alternative acquisition functions was considered beyond the scope of this project. 

A popular choice is the Expected Improvement of a candidate point by Močkus et al. \cite{Mockus1978}, which was also chosen as acquisition function of this project. Močkus et al. proposed to maximize the expected improvement with respect to the best value known thus far. The idea behind EI is to maximize the expected improvement relative to the best value observed so far. Since a minimization is performed, the improvement of a candidate point $x_{n+1}$ is defined as the decrease of the cost function value at $x_{n+1}$ relative to $f(x^{+})$, with $x^+ = \text{argmin}_{x_i \in \boldsymbol{X}} f(x_i)$. Because the objective is modeled by a Gaussian Process, this improvement is itself a random quantity:

The improvement of a candidate point $I(x_{n+1})$ is defined by the decrease (as we are performing a minimization) of the cost function in $f(x_{n+1})$ against $f(x^{+})$ with $x^+ = \text{argmin}_{x_i \in \boldsymbol{X}} f(x_i)$. The improvement is in itself a random quantity due to the Gaussian process model: 
\begin{equation}\label{eq::improvement}
    \tilde{I}(x_{n+1}) = \max\{0,\; f(x^+) - \tilde{f}(x_{n+1}) \},
\end{equation}
and the expected improvement follows as its expectation:
\begin{equation}
    EI(x_{n+1}) = \mathbb{E}\left[\tilde{I}(x_{n+1}) \,\middle|\, x_{n+1} \right].
\end{equation}

To calculate the expected improvement Močkus et al. provided this closed form:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::EI}
\begin{array}{ll}
E I(\hat{\mathbf{x}}) & =\tilde{\Sigma}_{\ell}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})(Z \Phi(Z)+\phi(Z)) \\
& \text { with: } Z=\frac{\tilde{\mu}_{\ell}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})-\ell\left(\mathbf{x}^{+}\right) - \xi}{\tilde{\Sigma}_{\ell}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})},
\end{array}
\end{equation}
in which $\Phi$ and $\phi$ denote the cumulative distribution function and probability density function of the standard normal distribution, respectively \cite{gardner2014bayesian}. Where $\xi$ is introduced by Lizotte \cite{Lizotte2008} as an extra parameter to improve the trade-off towards more exploration in stead of exploitation. 

\subsubsection{Constrained Acquisition Function}\label{sec::constacq}
Up to now, we have only looked at unconstrained Bayesian Optimization. In order to extend the Bayesian Optimization approach incorporating inequality constraints, the problem becomes:
\begin{mini!}|s|
  {x}
  {f(x)}
  {}
  {}
  \addConstraint{c(x) \leq \lambda}{}
\end{mini!}
where $f(x)$ and $c(x)$ are both expensive functions to evaluate. In many cases, both function values can be computed from the same experimental outcome. Implicit methods that include the constraint within the cost function, such as barrier functions, were briefly considered. However, with these methods, the cost increases drastically near the constraint boundary, which prevents any sampling in that region. As the optimum is expected to lie close to this boundary, such an implicit approach would steer the optimization away from precisely the region of interest, and it was therefore not examined further. Instead, this research applies the constrained BO formulation of Gardner et al. \cite{gardner2014bayesian}, which treats the constraint explicitly, outside of the cost function by extending the Expected Improvement acquisition function. It was moreover the only approach that could be implemented using the software package selected for this project, namely the Python package \texttt{bayesian-optimization} \cite{BOpackage}. This choice of software is discussed further in Section \ref{sec::BOimplementation}.

In this formulation, the constraint enters the BO loop through a modification of the EI acquisition function, which requires two adjustments. First, $x^+$ is redefined as the feasible sampling point with the lowest observed objective value in $f(\boldsymbol{X})$. Second, infeasible candidates are assigned zero improvement. Together, this yields the constrained improvement function for a candidate point $x_{n+1}$:

\begin{equation}
    I_c(x_{n+1}) = \Delta(x_{n+1})\, \max\{0,\; f(x^+) - f(x_{n+1})\} = \Delta(x_{n+1})\, I(x_{n+1}),
\end{equation}

where $\Delta(x_{n+1}) \in \{0,1\}$ indicates feasibility: it equals $1$ when $c(x_{n+1}) \leq \lambda$ and $0$ when the constraint is violated.

The constraint function $c(x)$ is modeled analogously to the objective, namely with a Gaussian Process that is assumed to be conditionally independent of the GP of $f(x)$, given $x$. After a new sampling point $x_{n+1}$ has been selected and evaluated, the observed values of $f$ and $c$ are used to update their respective posteriors. Under this probabilistic model, the constrained improvement becomes a random quantity:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{I}_c(x_{n+1}) = \tilde{\Delta}(x_{n+1})\, \max\{0,\; f(x^+) - \tilde{f}(x_{n+1})\} = \tilde{\Delta}(x_{n+1})\, \tilde{I}(x_{n+1}),
\end{equation}

with $\tilde{\Delta}(x)$ as a Bernoulli random variable with parameter:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::bernoulli}
\operatorname{PF}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}):=\operatorname{Pr}[\tilde{c}(\mathbf{x}) \leq \lambda]=\int_{-\infty}^\lambda p\left(c(x_{n+1}) \mid x_{n+1}, \mathcal{T}_c\right) d c(x_{n+1})
\end{equation}
Since the posterior $\tilde{c}(x_{n+1})$ is marginally Gaussian, $\mathrm{PF}(x_{n+1})$ can be computed by evaluating a single univariate Gaussian cumulative distribution function. Taking the expectation of the constrained improvement then yields the expected constrained improvement acquisition function:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::addingconstraint2EI}
\begin{aligned}
EI_c(x_{n+1}) &= \mathbb{E}\left[\tilde{I}_c(x_{n+1}) \mid x_{n+1}\right] \\
&= \mathbb{E}\left[\tilde{\Delta}(x_{n+1})\, \tilde{I}(x_{n+1}) \mid x_{n+1}\right] \\
&= \mathbb{E}\left[\tilde{\Delta}(x_{n+1}) \mid x_{n+1}\right]\, \mathbb{E}\left[\tilde{I}(x_{n+1}) \mid x_{n+1}\right] \\
&= \mathrm{PF}(x_{n+1})\, EI(x_{n+1}),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the factorization of the expectation in the third step is justified by the assumed conditional independence of $c(x)$ and $f(x)$ given the input. The resulting acquisition function therefore has an intuitive interpretation: it is the standard expected improvement with respect to the best feasible sample found so far, weighted by the probability that the candidate point satisfies the constraint.

Up to now, only a single inequality constraint has been considered. The approach of Gardner et al. can also handle multiple inequality constraints of the form $\boldsymbol{c}(x) \leq \boldsymbol{\Lambda}$, with $\boldsymbol{c}(x) = [c_1(x), \dots, c_k(x)]$ and $\boldsymbol{\Lambda} = [\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_k]$. The constraints are added to the acquisition function by redefining the Bernoulli parameter of $\tilde{\Delta}(x_{n+1})$ as the probability that all constraints are satisfied at once:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{PF}(x_{n+1}) = \Pr\left[\tilde{c}_1(x_{n+1}) \leq \lambda_1, \dots, \tilde{c}_k(x_{n+1}) \leq \lambda_k\right],
\end{equation}
while the rest of the expected constrained improvement in Equation \ref{eq::addingconstraint2EI} stays the same. This joint probability is now a multivariate Gaussian, which can be computed using available numerical methods \cite{gardner2014bayesian}. When the constraints are assumed to be conditionally independent given the sampling point, the probability simplifies into a product of univariate Gaussian CDFs, $\prod_{i=1}^{k} \Pr[\tilde{c}_i(x_{n+1}) \leq \lambda_i]$, making the evaluation just as cheap as with a single constraint.

\section{Problem definition}
The optimization problem is defined as follows:

\begin{mini!}|s|
  {\boldsymbol{\theta}}
  {\sum_{k=k_w}^{N-1} \Big[\, w_1\, \dot{m}_s(k)\, c_p \big(T_s(k) - 20\big)
      + w_2\, \dot{m}_r(k)\, c_p \big(T_r(k) - 10 \big) \Big] \Delta k}
  {}{}
  \addConstraint{\boldsymbol{x}(k+1)}{= f\!\left(\boldsymbol{x}(k-\tau(k)\!:\!k), \boldsymbol{u}(k), \boldsymbol{d}(k) \right),}{\quad k = 0,\ldots,N-1}
  \addConstraint{\boldsymbol{u}(k)}{= \left[T_s(k),\, h(k)\right]^{\top}}{}
  \addConstraint{\boldsymbol{d}(k)}{= \boldsymbol{Q}_d(k)}{}
  \addConstraint{T_s(k)}{= f_1\!\left(Q_{\text{d,tot}}(k),\, \theta_1, \ldots, \theta_4\right)}{}
  \addConstraint{h(k+1)}{= f_2\!\left(T_{\text{of}}(k),\, \theta_5, \theta_6\right)}{}
  \addConstraint{\operatorname{softmax}_k T_r(k)}{\leq T_{r,\max}}{}\label{eq::Trcons}
  \addConstraint{\Delta Q_{\text{tot}}}{\leq (1+\epsilon)\, \Delta Q_{\text{tot,b}}}{}\label{eq::deltaQcons}
  \addConstraint{\Delta Q_{\text{resp,max}}}{\leq (1+\gamma)\, \Delta Q_{\text{resp,max,b}}}{}\label{eq::Qrespcons}
  \addConstraint{\boldsymbol{x}(0)}{= \left[T_{\text{amb}}\mathbf{1}_{N_n}^{\top},\; \dot{m}_0\mathbf{1}_{N_e}^{\top}\right]^{\top}}{}
  % \addConstraint{(\boldsymbol{x}(k),\, \boldsymbol{\theta})}{\in \mathcal{X} \times \Theta}{}
\end{mini!}

where $T_s(k)$ and $T_r(k)$ are respectively the supply and return water temperature of the network, $\dot{m}_s(k)$ and $\dot{m}_r(k)$ the supply and return mass flow rates, $w_1$ and $w_2$ are weighting factors and $\Delta k$ is the discretization timestep. The summation starts at $k_w$ once the initialization warm-up is complete. The state vector $\boldsymbol{x}(k) \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{N}| + |\mathcal{E}|} $ contains the temperatures of all the nodes and the inlet mass flow rates of all edges for timestep $k$.

\[
\boldsymbol{x}(k) =
\left[
\begin{array}{c}
T_{1}(k) \\
T_{2}(k) \\
\vdots \\
T_{|\mathcal{N}|}(k) \\
\dot{m}_1(k) \\
\dot{m}_2(k) \\
\vdots \\
\dot{m}_{|\mathcal{E}|}(k)
\end{array}
\right]
% \quad \boldsymbol{x}(k) \in \mathcal{X}
\]
The maximum memory depth of the state vector required by the Node method is $\tau(k)$, and is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{gathered}
        \varepsilon_{e}(k) = \min_{n} \left\{ n \geq 0,\; n \in \mathbb{Z} : \sum_{i=k-n}^{k} \dot{m}_e(i)\,\Delta t > \rho A_e L_e + \dot{m}_e(k)\,\Delta t \right\} \\[6pt]
        \tau(k) = \max_{e \in \mathcal{E}} \; \varepsilon_e(k)
    \end{gathered}
    \label{eq::gammadelay_min}
\end{equation}

The inputs of the system $u(k)$ at time step $k$ consist of the array of the heat demand of all the consumers $\boldsymbol{Q}_d(k) \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{N}_{\text{hex}}|}$, and the control inputs. 

\[
\boldsymbol{Q}_d(k) =
\left[
\begin{array}{c}
Q_{1}(k) \\
Q_{2}(k) \\
\vdots \\
Q_{|\mathcal{N}_{\text{hex}}|}(k)
\end{array}
\right]
% \quad \boldsymbol{x}(k) \in \mathcal{X}
\]

The optimization variable $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is used to parameterize the control inputs $T_s$ and $h$ via piece-wise affine functions $f_1: \mathbb{R}^5 \rightarrow [\theta_1,\theta_2]$ and $f_{2}: \mathbb{R}^3 \rightarrow [0,1]$. The definition of these piece-wise affine functions is further discussed in Section \ref{sec::controlInputs}. The constraint definitions are explained in Section \ref{sec::optconstraints}.

\subsubsection{Objective function}
The goal of the control policy is to lower the return temperature while still sufficiently meeting the heat demand and the response requirements. Lowering the return temperature primarily serves to prevent heat pump failure caused by excessive return temperatures. Even when no failure occurs, a high return temperature still requires more energy from the heat pump to store water in the hot well of the ATES. This is simplified to $\dot{m}_r(k)\, c_p \big(T_r(k) - 10\big)$, where the hot ATES well is assumed to be at a constant temperature of $10\,^{\circ}$C. Since this term only penalizes high return temperatures rather than strictly preventing them, an explicit constraint on the return temperature is added as well, which is discussed in Section \ref{sec::optconstraints}.

In addition to the energy required for storage, the energy supplied to the network is included in the cost function, ensuring that the complete energy consumption of the DCS is addressed. During summer operation, the hot water is not extracted from the ATES but is instead supplied by the backup district heating network. The energy delivered by this network to heat the supply water is simplified to $\dot{m}_s(k)\, c_p \big(T_s(k) - 20\big)$, where it is assumed that the water is heated starting from the ambient temperature of $20\,^{\circ}$C.

To prevent the heat pumps from failing, an explicit constraint is imposed on the return temperature in \eqref{eq::Trcons}, where the maximum return temperature during the simulation is obtained by performing a softmax operation. Here, $T_{r,\max}$ is defined as the temperature at which the heat pumps break down. The other two constraints focus on how well the system provides its consumers with heat and how fast it does so. To evaluate this, the performance of the optimized simulation is compared with the performance of the system using benchmark actuators. For this benchmark simulation, the model for realistic bypass operation presented in \cite{BRAND2014256} is adopted. In this model, the thermostat maintains the water temperature around the setpoint by applying a deadband of width $T_{\text{db}}$, opening the valve at $T_{\text{set}} - \frac{T_{\text{db}}}{2}$ and closing it at $T_{\text{set}} + \frac{T_{\text{db}}}{2}$. Formulating the heat demand constraints based on the heat response guideline used by Eneco turned out to be difficult. This guideline states how long it may take for a consumer to obtain water of $45\,^{\circ}$C at the tap. However, this presumes that every consumer fully opens their tap whenever hot water is required, which is not the case in normal operation. Moreover, the guideline only indicates whether the requirement was met or not, and gives no information on how far the temperature deviates from the setpoint. Therefore, the heat demand response requirements are formulated in a different way.

To capture the demand response requirement, the heat response of consumer $i$, $\Delta Q_{\text{resp},i}$, is introduced. It is defined as the heat deficit during the first two minutes after consumer $i$ increases its heat demand. Only deficits are taken into account, which is achieved by applying a softrelu operation on the difference between heat demand and heat supply:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::Qresp}
    \Delta Q_{\text{resp},i} = \sum_{n = n_w}^{N-1} \bigg[\big(Q_{d,i}(n) - Q_{d,i}(n-1)\big)
      \operatorname{softrelu}\bigg(\sum_{k=n}^{n+n_{\text{2min}}} \big(Q_{d,i}(k) - Q_{s,i}(k)\big)\bigg)\bigg],
\end{equation}
where $n_{\text{2min}}$ is the number of timesteps corresponding to two minutes. Subsequently, a softmax operation is performed on the vector $\boldsymbol{\Delta Q}_{\text{resp}} \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{N}_{\text{hex}}|}$ containing the heat responses of all consumers, resulting in the worst-case response $\Delta Q_{\text{resp,max}}$ used in \eqref{eq::Qrespcons}.

Besides the response to changes in demand, it also matters how well the heat demand is met in general. This is captured by the total heat deficit over the simulation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::Qtot}
      \Delta Q_{\text{tot}} = \sum_{n = n_w}^{N-1} \big(Q_{\text{d,tot}}(n)-Q_{\text{s,tot}}(n)\big)^2.
\end{equation}

There is no strict rule regarding how well heat demand requirements must be met relative to the benchmark performance. Therefore, $\epsilon$ and $\gamma$ are introduced in \eqref{eq::deltaQcons} and \eqref{eq::Qrespcons} to define how much the optimized simulation may deviate from the benchmark values. Their specific values will be determined using 5\% steps, beginning at 25\%, with each subsequent step decreasing by 5\%.


\subsubsection{Control inputs}\label{sec::controlInputs}
The simulation spans a full day, optimizing the supply temperature and thermostatic bypass valve displacement at every individual timestep would produce thousands of decision variables, a scale for which Bayesian Optimization is poorly suited. Instead, a lumped optimization approach is adopted, reducing the problem to a handful of parameters. The thermostatic bypass valve is modeled following the P-band relation present in the FJVR. The supply temperature controller is modeled following the heating curves. These control curves are conventionally used in district heating networks to couple ambient temperature to supply temperature, as it roughly reflects heating demand \cite{heatingCurve,gradyent2024heatingcurves}. Assuming full knowledge of the heating demand, the supply temperature is directly coupled to the DCS's total heating demand. 

This reduces the optimization variables to $\boldsymbol{\theta} = \left[\theta_1, ..., \theta_6 \right]$, which are listed below along with their graphical representations.
\begin{align*}

    \theta_5 &: \text{Bypass valve temperature setpoint} [^{\circ}\text{C}] \\

\section{Implementation of Bayesian optimization} \label{sec::BOimplementation}
This section discusses parameter settings of the Bayesian Optimization implementation. It is implemented using the Python package 'bayesian-optimization' because it provides a user-friendly interface and is well-documented. 

\subsection{Kernel}
The package uses a Mat\'ern  covariance kernel, which is a common choice within BO \cite{bo_tutorial}. The Mat\'ern kernel is defined as \cite{rasmussen2006gaussian}:

\begin{equation}
k\left(x_i, x_j\right)=\frac{1}{\Gamma(\nu) 2^{\nu-1}}\left(\frac{\sqrt{2 \nu}}{l} d\left(x_i, x_j\right)\right)^\nu K_\nu\left(\frac{\sqrt{2 \nu}}{l} d\left(x_i, x_j\right)\right),
\end{equation}

with $d(\cdot,\cdot)$ as the Euclidean distance,  $Kv(\cdot)$ the modified Bessel function and $\Gamma (\cdot)$ the gamma function. To handle the different parameter ranges of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ and the model sensitivity per input parameter, an anisotropic formulation is used. Resulting in separate length scales per input dimension. The input parameter $v$ controls the smoothness of the resulting Gaussian Process. The smaller $\nu$, the smoother the GP definition. In this work, $\nu = 5/2$ is used, which implies that the objective function will be recreated using a twice differentiable Gaussian Process. Even though the objective is not smooth everywhere, the simulation exhibits a discontinuity at zero mass flow rate in the Node method described at the end of Section \ref{sec::NodeMethod}, and the lumped control functions introduce non-differentiable points. It was assumed that the Mat\'ern 5/2 kernel could still be applied, as the Gaussian Process will approximate these regions with a smooth interpolation. A more sophisticated determination of the kernel might yield better optimization results; however, due to the thesis's time constraints, it is outside its scope.

% \begin{equation}\label{eq::matern}
%     k(x, x') = \sigma_f^2 \, \frac{2^{1-\nu}}{\Gamma(\nu)} \left( \sqrt{2\nu}\, r \right)^{\!\nu} K_\nu\!\left( \sqrt{2\nu}\, r \right), \qquad r = \sqrt{\sum_{d=1}^{D} \frac{(x_d - x_d')^2}{l_d^2}},
% \end{equation}
% where $\sigma_f^2$ is the signal variance, $\Gamma$ the gamma function, and $K_\nu$ the modified Bessel function of the second kind. An anisotropic formulation is used, in which each of the $D$ input dimensions has its own lengthscale $l_d$, such that the model can adapt its sensitivity per input parameter. The smoothness parameter $\nu$ controls the differentiability of the functions drawn from the GP prior: sample paths are $\lceil \nu \rceil - 1$ times differentiable. In this work, $\nu = 5/2$ is used, for which the kernel reduces to the simple closed form:
% \begin{equation}\label{eq::matern52}
%     k(x, x') = \sigma_f^2 \left( 1 + \sqrt{5}\, r + \tfrac{5}{3} r^2 \right) \exp\!\left( -\sqrt{5}\, r \right),
% \end{equation}
% corresponding to a prior over twice-differentiable functions. 

The length scales $l$ used for the kernels of both the objective function and the constraints are fitted at every iteration by maximizing the log marginal likelihood \cite{rasmussen2006gaussian}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::lml}
    \log p\!\left(f(\boldsymbol{X}) \mid \boldsymbol{X}, \boldsymbol{l}\right) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\, f(\boldsymbol{X})^{\top} \left( K + \alpha I \right)^{-1} f(\boldsymbol{X}) - \tfrac{1}{2} \log \left| K + \alpha I \right| - \tfrac{n}{2} \log 2\pi,
\end{equation}
with $K$ as the covariance matrix $k(\boldsymbol{X},\boldsymbol{X})$. 

% The length scales of the constraint GPs are not optimized, as each additional constraint would further increase the number of hyperparameters to be fitted, which, given the small number of samples, could lead to overfitting. Instead, the lengthscale of each constraint GP is fixed per dimension to the range of the corresponding input parameter.

The term $\alpha I$ in Equation \eqref{eq::lml} represents the level of observation noise the Gaussian Process can handle, which the package defines via the parameter $\alpha$. It enters the model as additive Gaussian noise on the diagonal of the covariance matrix:
\begin{equation}\label{eq::nugget}
    \tilde{K} = K + \alpha I.
\end{equation}
This term, known as the nugget, is generally used to account for noise in the function evaluations. A second purpose it serves is to improve the conditioning of the covariance matrix, preventing it from becoming numerically singular when sampled points lie close together. For this reason, the default value of $\alpha = 10^{-6}$ is retained even though no noise is modeled and the model is deterministic. 

\subsection{Acquisition Function}
Following the constrained Bayesian Optimization implementation of Gardner et al. \cite{gardner2014bayesian} discussed in Section \ref{sec::constacq}, the Expected Improvement acquisition function is applied, weighted by the probability of feasibility of the candidate point. Expected Improvement was chosen because it is the acquisition function for which this constrained formulation is derived.

\section{Summary}
To handle the non-convexity within the developed simulation model, caused by, for instance, Kirchhoff's second law, it was decided to make use of Bayesian Optimization. Bayesian Optimization is used to find the extreme of functions that are expensive to evaluate and is considered highly efficient considering the number of samples needed to find the optimum. It incorporates a prior belief about the objective function, which helps in sampling the data points by the acquisition function. This prior is combined with the likelihood function obtained after each sample, yielding a posterior over the objective. To incorporate constraints into the optimization, the approach of Gardner et al. \cite{gardner2014bayesian} uses the expected constrained improvement acquisition function. 

The cost function is formulated as a simplified representation of the amount of energy used by the heat pumps within the DCS. The control inputs are six parameters that define lumped relations between the supply temperature and the thermostatic bypass valve. The optimization is constrained by a maximum allowed return temperature and two constraints comparing the heat demand response and total heat demand of the optimized simulation with a benchmark.

Depending on the type of system you want to optimize, the kernel might vary. A common example of a kernel is the squared exponential kernel (\ref{eq::isotropic}). With $\theta$ as a hyperparameter affecting the width of the kernel. In the case of anisotropic models, this hyperparameter becomes a diagonal matrix \cite{rasmussen2006gaussian}. The entries of $diag(\theta)$ control how much influence the features have over the covariance.
