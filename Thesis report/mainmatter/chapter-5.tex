\chapter{Results}
\label{chapter:Results}

This Chapter presents the results of the constrained Bayesian Optimization applied to a full-day simulation compared with the defined benchmark simulation. Before presenting the control results, the simulation settings, the ranges of the control inputs, and the fixed values of two control inputs are described.

\section{Simulation settings}
Besides the constants presented in Section~\ref{sec::physicalConstants}, the following simulation parameters are applied. The ambient temperature is set constant at 20\,°C, and the initial temperature of the water and the pipes is set equal to this ambient temperature and the maximum pump pressure is set at 50 kPa. Furthermore, the simulation timestep $\Delta k$ is set to 1 second in order to always conform to the CFL condition. 

The network used for the simulations is similar to the network used by Eneco for its in-house steady-state model of the heat distribution within a DCS project. The simulated network represents a 23-floor building, with the floors spaced 3 m apart. At every floor, a heat exchanger is connected to the riser at a distance of 2.5 m from the junction, using DN20 piping. The supply and return risers themselves are divided into three sections with decreasing diameter along the height of the building: DN40 for the lowest 6 floors, DN32 for the following 14 floors, and DN25 for the top 3 floors. The parameters of the four pipe types were presented in Tables~\ref{tab:pipe_dn_common} and~\ref{tab:pipe_dn_geometry}.

For the optimization, both weights $w_1$ and $w_2$ are set equal to 
$\frac{1}{4181} \cdot 10^{-3}$, thereby equally prioritizing both terms. Scaling down the weights in this manner keeps the resulting values of the cost function in a readable range. The warm-up timestep $k_w$, after which the simulation results are included in the cost function, is set equivalent to 6 hours. This duration corresponds to the time required for the sensor temperature within the thermostatic bypass valve to stabilize, as shown in Section~\ref{sec::appendixbypasstemp}.

\section{Control inputs}
While performing the optimization, the control inputs $\theta_4$ and $\theta_6$ 
appeared to not influence the objective function. This was indicated by 
the size of their fitted length scales: during the fitting of the Gaussian 
Process, the length scales are allowed to vary between 0.05 and 3 times the 
range of their respective input parameter, and for both $\theta_4$ and 
$\theta_6$ the fitted length scale reached this upper bound. Such a large 
length scale implies that the objective function barely varies over the entire 
parameter range, indicating that the parameter has almost no effect within 
its bounds. Based on this, it was decided to fix $\theta_6$ at 3 $^{\circ}C$.

In the case of $\theta_4$, it was decided to verify that the Gaussian Process correctly captured the shape of the objective function with respect to $\theta_4$; a parameter sweep was performed. Since these sweeps are computationally expensive, the sweeps were only performed for Profile 2 to serve as an indication. For comparison, sweeps over $\theta_2$, $\theta_3$ and $\theta_5$ were performed as well, showing the impact of these parameters relative to that of $\theta_4$. These sweeps were used to get a sense of the effects of these parameters on the objective function and on the constraints. Table \ref{tab::sweep_theta4} shows the change in the objective function or the constraints over the full range of these control inputs and confirms the negligible influence of $\theta_4$. The plots of the sweeps are presented in Chapter~\ref{chapter::appendixExtraPlots}. These results confirm the negligible influence of $\theta_4$ on the objective function and the variables which are constrained. Therefore, it was decided to fix $\theta_4$ at the halfway point of its profile-dependent range.

\section{Control results}
This section presents the results of the constrained Bayesian optimization applied to a full-day simulation compared with the defined benchmark simulation. Three tolerance values, $\alpha = \beta \in \{0.05, 0.15, 0.25\}$ are considered, each applied to simulations using the four heat demand profiles introduced in Section~\ref{sec::heatdemand}. In addition to the plots of the optimized simulations, the optimized control input values and the fitted length scales of the Gaussian Process are shown. The length scale values presented in $\boldsymbol{\text{bold}}$ are at the minimum or maximum bound of the allowed length scale. The fitted length scale values of the constraint GPs can be found in Chapter ~\eqref{chapter::appendixExtraPlots}.
In the case of heat demand Profile 1, the optimization using the tolerance value of 0.05 failed because it could not find a feasible point, and the initial probe point was infeasible as well.

\section{Analysis of the Control Results}
Several observations can be made concerning the obtained results. First, an increase in the tolerance leads to a decrease of $\theta_5$ across all four heat demand profiles. This is as expected, since the temperature setpoint of the thermostatic bypass valve plays an important role in satisfying the heat demand constraints, which is also reflected in the length scales of the constraint GPs.

A decrease in the tolerance, on the other hand, pushes $\theta_1$ and 
$\theta_2$ towards their upper bound of 65\,°C. This too is expected: to 
comply with the tighter constraints, the supply temperature cannot remain 
relatively low, as the thermostatic bypass valve would then be unable to 
close the gap to the required heat demand.

The objective GP length scale of $\theta_3$ lies at either its lower or upper bound for most of the optimized simulations. A length scale at the lower bound indicates that the Gaussian Process interprets the parameter as noise, whereas a length scale at the upper bound indicates that it does not affect the objective function. This might suggest that $\theta_3$ could be fixed as well. However, the length scales of the constraint GPs show that $\theta_3$ still plays a role in satisfying the heat demand constraints for the first two profiles. For Profiles 3 and 4, it has no effect at all, which is attributed to the lower simultaneity of the heat demand: the change in demand is less abrupt, covering the heat demand range more densely and thereby reducing the influence 
of $\theta_3$.

A similar observation holds for $\theta_5$, whose objective GP length scale is frequently at its maximum value. Nevertheless, $\theta_5$ plays a significant role in satisfying the constraints, and it is therefore kept as a control input.

For Profile 4, none of the control inputs appear to influence the 
objective function value. This may be explained by the more spread-out heat demand of this profile: the pipes within the network remain warmer, such that changes within the control input ranges have little effect. The constraint GP length scales, however, indicate that $\theta_1$ and $\theta_5$ do influence whether the feasible region is reached. The exact cause of these length scale values would require a closer look at the network behavior under Profile 4.

As a final remark, the return temperature in all profiles approaches 15\,°C, which is extraordinarily low. This behavior is caused by the $\epsilon$-NTU method. When the consumers start demanding heat, the valves open only gradually, resulting in very low mass flows. Since the NTU method assumes that the water passes through the heat exchanger within a single timestep, a large amount of energy is extracted from this small mass of water. As the water in the pipeline is around 20\,°C, the combination of a low mass flow and this high extracted energy per unit mass leads to a dip to very low temperatures. To handle this behavior, a dynamic model of the heat exchanger should be implemented that takes into account the traveling time through the heat exchanger.
