\chapter{Discussion}\label{chapter:Discussion}
The discussion of this thesis project is split into two parts: one concerning the developed simulation model, and one concerning the control policy implemented through constrained Bayesian Optimization.

\section{Simulation model}
One of the main discussion points regarding the developed simulation model is the small timestep of 1 second. This is a consequence of the Node Method being an explicit approach to solving the PDE of the water temperature within the pipeline. The CFL condition forces the timestep down to 1 second. This puts the steady-state assumptions under additional pressure and causes the simulation to take considerably more time than it would with larger timesteps. In addition, the Node Method is not defined for zero mass flow, so an extra assumption had to be introduced to handle this case. A method that handles mass flow rate of zero natively would therefore be preferable.

The steady-state assumptions of the Effectiveness-NTU method used to model the heat exchangers do not hold at very low mass flow rates, since the method assumes that the water passes through the heat exchanger within a single timestep. To handle this behavior more realistically, a dynamic heat exchanger model should be implemented that accounts for the travel time of the water through the heat exchanger.

Similarly, the small timestep challenges the idealized representation of the valves, as real valves exhibit hysteresis-like behavior. Although the effect of such valve imperfections would largely average out at the level of the whole system, on the scale of an individual valve the behavior could deviate from reality. Since the influence of the thermostatic bypass valve is particularly large during periods of low heat demand, this could still affect the overall supply and return temperature behavior.

Furthermore, the scaling of the heat transfer coefficient through the heat 
exchanger relies on the assumption that the flow through the heat exchanger is turbulent, which does not always hold. 

Another remark concerns the pump, which is modeled as operating on only a 
single head curve; a more sophisticated pump model would yield more realistic pump behavior. The same applies to the modeling of the supplied heat and the storage of energy in the ATES, while more realistic heat demand profiles would further improve the fidelity of the model.

\section{Control policy}
The initial warm-up time of the system is currently set at 6 hours. This was based on the time it takes for the sensor temperature of the thermostatic bypass valve to stabilize, but this does not imply that the water within the pipes and the pipes themselves have reached steady state. A longer simulation combined with a longer warm-up time would therefore be preferable, as it would reduce the influence of the initialization settings on the results.

Furthermore, no sensitivity analysis was performed for the Bayesian 
Optimization. As a result, it is not known what effect the deviations of the model with respect to reality have on the resulting control policy. Such an analysis could be carried out by adding noise to the obtained supply and return temperatures and observing how the Bayesian Optimization handles it. This would, however, require extending the Bayesian Optimization approach to handle noisy observations, for instance by adding a nugget to the Gaussian Processes.

The cost function is a heavily simplified representation of the total energy used by the DCS network. For instance, the efficiency of the heat pump, which depends on the return temperature, is not taken into account. Incorporating it was attempted earlier, but it drove the acquisition function persistently outside the feasible region. It should be noted, however, that in this attempt the length scales of the Gaussian Processes modeling the constraints were not yet fitted, so a renewed attempt with the fitted constraint GPs might yield better results.

Beyond this, there are many options to extend the cost function, as several aspects are arguably more important than the energy put into the system alone. The cost function could be expanded with terms accounting for the wear of the valves and other mechanical components, for the fact that a low temperature throughout the system is desired to increase its efficiency, or for the penalization of rapid changes in the supply temperature, which are likewise undesirable. This last term was in fact implemented, with a term penalizing the maximum change in supply temperature between timesteps. However, it proved too dominant within the cost function, and it was decided to simplify the cost function further in order to first obtain a working optimization before further trying to implement such a term.

% In theory, if the differential pressure across the valve remains constant, the flow characteristic w.r.t. the valve opening would exactly match the valve characteristic. However, in reality this is not the case as the differential pressure over the valve depends on the valve position. The distortion of the $K_v$ characteristic is defined by the valve authority ($\beta$), which is the ratio of the differential pressure over the valve when it is fully opened ($\Delta p_{v,h=1}$) and fully closed ($\Delta p_{v,h=0}$). In case the system is hydraulically balanced the differential pressure over the closed valve can be replaced by the pump pressure.

% The valve authority is generally stated as a dimensionless number between 0 and 1. The lower $\beta$, the greater the distortion. A reduction of the valve size would lead to a higher valve authority but it would dissipate too much of the pump energy. In practice, valve authority is typically designed to fall between 0.3 and 0.5, for which the resulting distortion is generally considered acceptable. 

% Besides the pressure changes caused by the displacement of the valve itself, other control valves or active components in the system induce pressure variations over the valve. This phenomenon is called hydraulic interactivity. Compensating for this change in pressure might lead to an oscillating valve behavior and instable control. One can counter such behavior by using a Differential Pressure Control Valve (DPCV) which ensures a constant differential pressure over the valve. The use of such a valve would be interesting in our case as the delivery sets contain dynamic control valves.
