\section{Background}
\subsection{Sustainable Heating}
The Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015, aims to limit global warming to well below 2$^{\circ}\text{C}$, and preferably to 1.5$^{\circ}\text{C}$ above pre-industrial levels. Achieving this goal requires significant de-carbonization across all sectors, including heating. Within the European Union in 2023, around 62.5 \% of household energy consumption is used for space heating. However, only 23.5 \% of that energy currently comes from renewable sources \cite{EUenergie}. While in the Netherlands 82 \% of homes were still heated by individual gas boilers in 2021 \cite{NLenergie}.
This share of renewable sources must increase substantially if we are to meet the goals set by the Paris Agreement. District Heating Networks (DHN) are considered a key solution in the de-carbonization of the heating sector, as they enable the integration of local renewable heat sources, renewable electricity, and waste heat \cite{KUNTUAROVA}.

\subsection{District Heating Networks}
Current district heating/cooling networks transport thermal energy by heated or cooled water through insulated pipes. These pipes are connected in an underground local network with one (or more) energy source(s), possibly storage, and buildings. The district heating (and cooling) networks facilitate the heating (and cooling) of these buildings. The temperature of the water flowing in and out of the building is respectively called the supply and return temperature. There are a separate supply and return networks with identical topology. 

District heating was commercially introduced in the late 19th century in the United States and at the beginning of the 20th century in Europe \cite{bookMax}. Currently, the use of district heating networks is increasing worldwide. The International Energy Agency predicts that 350 million housing units will be connected to a district heating network in 2030, accounting for about 20 $\%$ of the global space heating needs \cite{IEAheating}. In the first generation of district heating networks, heat was transported using steam, which led to high heat losses and too much corrosion due to condensation. Therefore, the second generation made use of pressurized water at 100 $^{\circ}\text{C}$, which still causes high heat losses \cite{FemkeJanssenLit}. Nowadays, the newest networks are of the fourth generation. These networks are implemented with a focus on decarbonization, in contrast to the previous three generations, which heavily relied on fossil fuels. The water temperature in these systems has a maximum of 60 - 70$^{\circ}\text{C}$. This was made possible by reducing heat loss of the buildings themselves and using floor heating, requiring a lower supply temperature \cite{FemkeJanssenLit}. This supply temperature lowers grid losses and makes it economically feasible to implement waste heat sources, such as excess heat from data centers. Heat pumps and solar collectors become more efficient as well, making it more suitable for implementing (seasonal) thermal energy storage. In the literature, a fifth generation of district heating networks is discussed. The extension of adding a cooling network is often mentioned in its definitions. However, the authors of the paper "\textit{Perspectives on fourth and fifth generation district heating}" conclude that it is a complementary technology, not a new generation of district heating networks \cite{4GDH5GDH}.

\subsection{Changing Legislation}
In contrast to the electricity or gas networks, district heating networks are not connected to a national grid. This grid connects all producers and consumers, enabling consumers to choose among energy providers. District heating networks are local networks connected to local heat producers. Narrowing the choice of energy providers. Most of the time, one energy provider owns and supplies the district heating networks, creating a monopoly. The Dutch government is working on new legislation, the "Collective Heat Act" (Wet Collectieve Warmte, Wcw), to reform these monopolies and regulate heating and cooling prices for these networks. The government wants to stimulate the development of heating networks and guarantee their affordability, sustainability, and reliability. One of the provisions of the law states that collective heating networks serving 1,500 or more connections must be majority-owned for at least 51 $\%$ by public entities such as municipalities, provinces, or the national government \cite{WcWsite}. The Wcw allows more flexibility for heating networks with fewer than 1,500 connections. These smaller systems can be owned and operated by private entities, provided that they obtain an exemption from the municipality. The proposed law
must still be approved by both the House of Representatives (Tweede Kamer) and the Senate (Eerste Kamer) before it comes into effect. This legislative process may lead to changes to the bill and introduces uncertainty for energy companies.

\subsection{Decentralized Solutions}
Eneco is the market leader in District Heating in the Netherlands, with 150.000 customers. They have 68 heating networks, mainly in and around the cities of Utrecht, The Hague, Rotterdam, and Amsterdam. These networks differ in size, ranging from a couple of hundred to 59.000 connections \cite{warmtenetwerkenEneco}. The profitability of large networks already faced challenges due to their high investment costs. With the additional uncertainty caused by the Wcw, Eneco decided to focus on building smaller networks, called Decentralized Solutions (DCS) \cite{nos2025warmtenetten}. \\

Decentralized Solutions are small local networks with up to 1,500 connections. These networks generate, store, and manage all their thermal energy near the consumer. They consist of consumers (households and larger users), one or more heat sources, often including a heat pump, ground-based heat and cold storage, and an underground pipeline network to connect them all. In some cases, the DCS is also connected to a larger nearby district heating system as a backup for the peak demand.

The heat and cold underground storage is an Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage (ATES). The ATES generally consists of a heat pump and two wells. These wells are sandy layers underground where it simultaneously stores and extracts thermal energy by heating or cooling the groundwater. These are called hot or cold wells, respectively. The cold water used to cool the network is heated during the process and stored in the hot well. The same holds for the hot water that is stored in the cold well after being used for heating. In summer, when the cold well is heated to above approximately 10 $^{\circ}\text{C}$, the heat pump is used as a backup cooling machine. In winter, the heat pump increases the temperature of the groundwater extracted from the hot well to around 60 degrees $^{\circ}\text{C}$ needed to heat the associated building. While during heating, the heat pump cools the used groundwater to 5-8 $^{\circ}\text{C}$ \cite{bloemendal2018hidden}. ATES addresses the seasonal mismatch between the availability and demand for heating and cooling in buildings. While it significantly reduces reliance on fossil fuels, the heat pump still requires a substantial amount of electricity to operate \cite{tudelft_ates_triplet}.

\subsection{Return temperature problem}\label{sec::Trproblem}
During the summer, demand for heat decreases significantly, resulting in higher return temperatures for Decentralized Solutions. Return temperatures can reach up to 45-55 $^{\circ}\text{C}$, which is considered excessively high. These high return temperatures cause the heat pump's efficiency to drop drastically, or even cause the heat pump to fail, as the required temperature drop to store it in the cold well becomes too large. It leads to higher electricity use, and in the event of a malfunctioning heat pump, Eneco must fall back on electric boilers, thereby jeopardizing seasonal energy storage and the DCS's sustainability. To prevent this situation, the return temperature needs to be lowered. \\

The root of this problem lies in the fact that Eneco needs to comply with standards for how quickly the client obtains hot water at the tap, the so-called response time. By law, a consumer must have tap water of 55 $^{\circ}\text{C}$ within two minutes once it completely opens the tap, but Eneco follows stricter guidelines of at least 45 $^{\circ}\text{C}$ at the tap after 30 seconds. In periods of high heat demand, it is relatively easy to meet these requirements because hot water is frequently circulating through the pipeline risers, so hot water is already high up in the building when a consumer starts demanding heat, keeping the response time within bounds. However, when demand is low or nonexistent, water flow in the risers decreases, causing the water in the pipes to cool. Now the hot water has to travel farther to reach the consumer, leading to slower response times. To prevent this from happening, all pipeline risers were fitted with a thermodynamic bypass valve at the top, where the supply network is directly connected to the return network. This valve maintains the temperature at the top of the riser at a setpoint, warming the entire pipe. Eneco sets this setpoint to 55 degrees, which enables it to reach the response time guidelines comfortably. During periods of low heat demand, the water passing through the bypass valve is not mixed with much cold water from the inactive heat exchangers, resulting in high return temperatures that trouble the heat pumps. 

To address the return temperature problem, Eneco needs a simulation model for its Decentralized Solution networks. Beyond this specific issue, the model should serve as a broader tool optimizing the design and operation of these networks. 
This is translated into the following requirements: 

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Capture the dynamic behavior of consumers and producers, the thermodynamic and hydraulic behavior of the network, and the effects of their interactions.
    \item Provide a structure to add new components or integrate different component designs.
    % \item Support scenario simulations of design choices.
    \item Support the testing of control strategies.
    \item Be developed for, and remain extendable by, Eneco's engineers. 
\end{enumerate}

The developed simulation model is subsequently used to design a control policy to lower the return temperature. It employs two actuators: the thermostatic bypass valve and the supply temperature. The supply temperature was added as an actuator because it can support the bypass valve in still meeting heat demand response time guidelines while lowering the return temperature. 

\section{Literature review on DHN simulation software and control policies}
This section presents the existing literature on DHN simulation software and control policies used to lower their return temperature by actuating the thermostatic bypass valve and the supply temperature.

\subsection{Simulation models}
Numerous tools exist for simulating energy systems, many of which are also applicable to district heating networks. A lot of the tools are commercial and require a paid license. However, Eneco prefers free simulation software, thereby shifting the main focus of this analysis to free alternatives. Given that Eneco uses Python for internal development, special attention was paid to open-source tools built in or compatible with Python. Nevertheless, to avoid overlooking well-established commercial alternatives, the paid programs WANDA from Deltares  \cite{deltaresWanda}, TRNSYS \cite{trnsys1975} and IDA ICE \cite{IDAICe} were also included in the analysis. WANDA was previously used in a master’s thesis by Femke Janssen on district heating networks \cite{FemkeJanssenLit}, and TRNSYS and IDA ICE are popular simulation tools for energy systems \cite{KUNTUAROVA}. The use of computational fluid dynamics (CFD) programs was briefly considered but deemed too detailed for this project. In the appendix chapter \ref{chapter:appendixTable}, a technical comparison of the evaluated tools is presented.  

An overview of possibly suitable simulation tools was presented in Table 2-1. The paid license tools IDA ICE and TRNSYS could have been of high interest due to their dynamic modeling capability. However, it was decided to proceed with a non-commercial program due to the high cost and the fact that these programs are not used at Eneco, which makes implementation more difficult. IDA ICE offers a free educational license, but it will no longer be valid once this thesis is completed. The second argument also holds for the programs EnergyPLAN \cite{EnergyPlan} and EnergyPlus \cite{EnergyPlus}. Besides the fact that EnergyPLAN uses an hourly timestep, EnergyPlus focuses on building thermal behavior without detailed network modeling. The most serious option of the non-Python tools was OpenModelica \cite{OpenModelica}, which is an open-source software tool that supports the Modelica language. Although the Modelica language is commonly used for DHN modeling \cite{KUNTUAROVA}, the OpenModelica documentation is not elaborate, making debugging more difficult. Since using it would also require Eneco employees to learn a new modeling language, we decided not to proceed with OpenModelica. Therefore, the focus was pointed towards Python-based open-source software. PyDHN \cite{PyDHN} and GridPenguin \cite{GridPenguin} support thermodynamic simulations. Nevertheless, PyDHN is still a beta version, and GridPenguin’s fixed timestep is 1 hour, making both tools unsuitable. Pandapipes \cite{pandapipes} offers a quasi-static approach without a fixed minimum timestep. While it is an interesting option, the time required to understand its code structure and the potential debugging time were considered greater than the time to develop our model from scratch. Therefore, it was decided to build the model from scratch in Python, using open-source projects as inspiration for its structure.

To meet Eneco's request for a simulation model while addressing the return temperature problem, this thesis develops a dynamic simulation model of the DCS networks. In addition to the potentially greater insight a dynamic model can offer compared to a static one, the most important reason for creating a dynamic model was the short time intervals of the heat demand response.

\subsection{Control policy}
\subsubsection{Bypass Flow Control}
Articles revolving around the control of thermostatic bypass valves in district heating networks are scarcely present in the literature. Proper control of the bypasses can play an important role in lowering the return temperature \cite{app15062982,VANDERMEULEN201845}. In \cite{VANDERMEULEN201845}, the authors propose a theoretical benchmark for the performance of bypass controllers and compare it with a thermostatic bypass valve. Where Li et al. \cite{DTUlibrary} show the impact of varying thermostatic bypass flows (installed at the branch ends) on the return temperature for different heat loads, but they do not perform an optimization. Brand et al. \cite{BRAND2014256} also investigated the effect of thermostatic bypass valves, comparing them with different bypass valve configurations for bathroom floor heating to improve the overall system's energy efficiency. They concluded that the thermostatic bypass could not be fully replaced by floor heating because the floor heating's flow was limited. In \cite{BENAKOPOULOS2021120928} Benakopoulos et al. do optimize thermostatic regulation valves to achieve a better return temperature, but these are connected to the radiators on the customer side. Rong et al. \cite{RONG2025116197} developed an optimization model to maximize the heat storage capacity and economic efficiency of the DHN by selectively placing bypasses and controlling the flow through them with valves. 

\subsubsection{Supply Temperature Control}
Supply temperature control maintains the network temperature at the required level by injecting or extracting the appropriate amount of heat. Traditional supply temperature control is often achieved via a weather-compensation technique that adjusts the supply temperature based on outdoor conditions. The weather compensation (or heating) curves can vary significantly across studies, and if the curves are incorrectly commissioned, they can perform worse than constant supply temperatures, resulting in higher return temperatures \cite{app15062982, LIAO200555}. Liao et al. \cite{LIAO200555} propose an adaptive control method that relies on real-time measurements of the space heat load to adjust the supply temperature. It results in better meeting the heat demand. Tol et al. \cite{TOL2021105} also make use of real-time data. They present a novel demand-responsive control strategy that reacts to changes in the difference between the return and supply temperatures at substations. This does not necessarily result in a lower return temperature than the weather compensation curves, but it reduces electricity use. The authors of \cite{papaKonstantikou} developed a controller that determines the minimum supply temperature based on heat-demand predictions and network time-delay calculations. 

\subsubsection{Choice of optimization method}
The previous sections discussed the current literature on lowering the return temperature using the two selected actuators. This section presents an argument for using Bayesian Optimization as a suitable control policy for the simulation model to be developed. 

The developed simulation model becomes a non-convex nonlinear system (see Chapter \ref{chapter:sysmodel}). The causes of the non-convexity are listed below:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item  The quadratic equality constraints imposed by Kirchhoff's second law \eqref{eq::secondKirchhoff}.
    \item The use of the varying time delay of the water masses in the pipes, \eqref{eq::gammadelay} and \eqref{eq::epsilondelay}, as time indexes.
    \item The bilinear terms introduced in the Node Method, \eqref{eq::averagestay}, \eqref{eq::NMTout}, \eqref{eq::Theatcapacity}, the temperature mixing of the nodes, \eqref{eq::TempMix}, and the $\epsilon-NTU$ implementation of the heat exchangers, \eqref{eq::epsilon}.
\end{enumerate}

The authors of \cite{ArtikelMax} handle the Kirchhoff constraints by introducing a convex relaxation. The model proposed in  \cite{nodeMethodbin} also performs convex relaxation on these constraints and their bilinear terms. Relaxing the problem from MINLP to eventually MILP. The mixed-integer relation arises from handling the non-convexity of the Node method by introducing an auxiliary binary variable for each possible time delay and for its bilinear terms. As the test network in this thesis project contains only 23 consumers, it already comprises at least 100 nodes. This would introduce the use of a couple of thousand binary variables, which is significantly more than the authors' use case of an integrated six-bus electricity and three-node district heating network. By introducing a constant time delay and adopting a linear representation of the DHN, the problem can be reformulated as an MILP. This simplification, however, is unrealistic, as the time delay is not constant in practice \cite{linearDHN}. In \cite{Heuristicmethod}, they handle the complexity imposed by the integer time delay by proposing a Bender decomposition algorithm. In this way, they reduce the Node method to a nonconvex NLP problem, but they encounter convergence issues in edge cases. 

Another option would be to not perform convex relaxations but to directly optimize the non-convex nonlinear system. The gradient and Hessian cannot be determined analytically because the Node method's use of the minimum time delay introduces discontinuities in the derivatives. Although methods exist to circumvent these discontinuities when computing numerical gradients, evaluating the objective function is computationally expensive. Consequently, approaches that rely on gradient and Hessian information are not considered. Other options would be, for example, Simulated Annealing or the Powell and Nelder-Mead methods. However, these methods also require many function evaluations. Here, Bayesian Optimization (BO) is particularly interesting, as it is one of the most efficient approaches in terms of the number of function evaluations required to find an optimum \cite{bo_tutorial}. Therefore, this thesis proceeds with applying the (constraint) Bayesian Optimization technique. 

A popular application of Bayesian Optimization has been the tuning of machine learning algorithm hyperparameters \cite{gardner2014bayesian}. But it can be found in a wide range of applications. For example, Pelz et al. \cite{Pelz} use Bayesian Optimization to address the non-convexity inherent in electron ptychography, enabling phase-sensitive imaging at atomic or near-atomic resolution. Neumann-Brosig et al. \cite{thermalNetworkBO} found that Bayesian Optimization significantly outperformed particle swarm optimization and genetic algorithm in terms of the computation time required for the thermal design optimization of an electronic circuit board layout. Nevertheless, the literature on Bayesian Optimization used in the context of district heating networks is scarce. The few articles that apply the technique use it to find the hyperparameters of an artificial neural network (ANN). Li et al. \cite{LIbayesian} use it to optimize their LSTM for short-term heating load forecasting, and Abokersh et al. \cite{ABOKERSH2020403} develop an ANN model based on BO to find an alternative for the computationally expensive heuristic optimization models used for solar district heating systems. The master's thesis by de Koning \cite{deKoning2020} uses BO to determine the hyperparameters of an LSTM model that accurately simulates large district heating networks, which are computationally expensive to simulate. The developed LSTM was not able to achieve a lower cost. Aslan et al. \cite{Aslan06062025} use BO to optimize the hyperparameters of boiler-efficiency degradation models in district heating and cooling substations, thereby increasing the efficiency and reliability of energy systems.

This leaves a gap in the research on the application of Bayesian Optimization as a control method in the district heating network field.  

\section{Problem statement and research questions}\label{sec::researhqes}
This thesis develops a dynamic simulation model of Eneco's Decentralized Solution models and provides a control policy using Bayesian Optimization to lower the network's return temperature by actuating the thermostatic bypass valve and adjusting the supply temperature. This problem statement gives rise to the following two research questions:

\begin{itemize}
  \renewcommand\labelitemi{} % removes the bullet
    \item \textbf{Q1: How to develop a dynamic simulation model that captures the thermodynamics and hydraulics of a small-scale heating network fulfilling Eneco's model requirements?}
\end{itemize}

Eneco's model requirements, as mentioned in Section \ref{sec::Trproblem}, are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Capture the dynamic behavior of consumers and producers, the thermodynamic and hydraulic behavior of the network, and the effects of their interactions.
    \item Provide a structure to add new components or integrate different component designs.
    % \item Support scenario simulations of design choices.
    \item Support the testing of control strategies.
    \item Be developed for, and remain extendable by, Eneco's engineers. 
\end{enumerate}
To further specify the first requirement, the developed simulation model should at least capture the thermodynamic and hydraulic behavior of at least the following components:
\begin{itemize}
\item Pipes
\item Valves
\item Thermostatic bypass valves
\item Heat exchangers
\item Centrifugal pumps
\end{itemize}

Since real measurement data is unavailable for direct model validation, the model is assessed using the following criteria:
                               
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Kirchhoff's hydraulic laws are satisfied.
    \item Energy conservation is upheld at every node.
    \item The solution to the thermodynamic pipeline PDE is validated against an existing model from the literature and, where possible, against real measurement data.
    \item All physical constants are within ranges reported in the literature.
    \item The CFL condition is satisfied throughout the simulation.
\end{enumerate}

% Beyond providing Eneco's engineers with greater insight into the inner workings of Decentralized Solutions, the model should also facilitate straightforward implementation 

\begin{itemize}
  \renewcommand\labelitemi{} % removes the bullet
    \item \textbf{Q2: How can Bayesian optimization be used to determine the optimal thermostatic bypass valve setting and supply temperature to minimize the return temperature in decentralized solutions while meeting the required heat demand response time?}
\end{itemize}

The control policy will be tested under different full-day heat demand scenarios that vary in the degree of heat-demand simultaneity. The results from the optimization should be compared with those from a version of the model using benchmark actuator controls. This benchmark needs to be defined. The same holds for defining the required heat demand response time as a constraint for the control policy.
